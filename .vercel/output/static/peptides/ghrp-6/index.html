GHRP-6

Also known as: Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide-6, SKF-110679, Growth Hormone-Releasing Hexapeptide

A first-generation growth hormone secretagogue that stimulates GH release through the ghrelin receptor. Less selective than newer alternatives with effects on cortisol and prolactin. Historically significant as the peptide that led to ghrelin discovery. Cuban CIGB leads global cytoprotective research. Not approved for any indication.

Overview

Quick Summary

  • First-generation synthetic growth hormone secretagogue binding to ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a)
  • Historically significant: Led to discovery of ghrelin and the GHS receptor system through “reverse pharmacology”
  • Not FDA approved for any indication; development discontinued in favor of more selective compounds
  • Less selective than newer alternatives—increases cortisol and prolactin in addition to GH
  • Strong appetite stimulation (orexigenic effect) due to ghrelin receptor activation
  • Emerging cytoprotective research: Cardioprotective and neuroprotective effects under investigation
  • Limited human evidence: Primarily pharmacology/PK studies; no completed Phase 3 efficacy trials

Evidence Strength: LOW — Human pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic studies exist demonstrating GH-releasing activity, but no large efficacy trials for clinical endpoints. Development discontinued in favor of more selective compounds like ipamorelin. Emerging research on cytoprotective properties is primarily preclinical.

Why GHRP-6 Was Developed

In 1976, Cyril Bowers and colleagues observed that certain methionine-enkephalin derivatives lacking opioid activity could stimulate growth hormone release. This serendipitous discovery led to systematic structure-activity studies.

Development rationale:

  1. Opioid scaffold optimization: Modified met-enkephalin backbone to eliminate opioid effects while enhancing GH release
  2. D-amino acid incorporation: Unnatural D-Trp and D-Phe residues improved receptor binding and metabolic stability
  3. Conformational design: Computational energy calculations guided hexapeptide structure
  4. Result: GHRP-6 emerged as a potent, consistent GH secretagogue suitable for research

Historical importance: GHRP-6’s existence as an “orphan ligand” (a drug without a known receptor) drove research that ultimately identified the GHS-R1a receptor in 1996 and its natural ligand ghrelin in 1999. This represents a classic example of “reverse pharmacology”—synthetic compounds leading to discovery of endogenous systems.

Mechanism of Action

How GHRP-6 Works

GHRP-6 triggers growth hormone release by activating the same receptor that the “hunger hormone” ghrelin uses. Here’s what that means:

  1. Activates the ghrelin receptor - Your body has a receptor called GHS-R1a that responds to ghrelin (the hormone that makes you hungry). GHRP-6 was created before scientists even knew ghrelin existed, which is why it’s historically significant.

  2. Causes GH release - When GHRP-6 binds to the ghrelin receptor on cells in your pituitary gland, it triggers them to release growth hormone into your bloodstream.

  3. Not selective - Unlike newer peptides like ipamorelin, GHRP-6 also increases cortisol, prolactin, and appetite. This is considered a disadvantage for most applications but may be useful in specific situations like treating wasting diseases.

  4. May have protective effects - Emerging research suggests GHRP-6 might protect heart and brain tissue from damage, though this is mostly studied in animals.

Historical importance: GHRP-6’s discovery in 1984 led scientists to hunt for the natural substance the body uses to activate this receptor. This search resulted in the discovery of ghrelin in 1999, a classic example of a drug leading to the discovery of a new hormone system.

Development status: GHRP-6 was never approved because less selective compounds aren’t ideal for most therapeutic uses. Newer, more selective alternatives were developed instead.

Receptor Targets

GHRP-6 binds to two distinct receptors with different biological consequences:

Primary: Ghrelin Receptor (GHS-R1a)

Receptor Classification (IUPHAR/BPS Guide to Pharmacology):

  • Official name: Ghrelin receptor (GHSR)
  • Gene: GHSR (human); Ghsr (mouse/rat)
  • Class: Class A G protein-coupled receptor (7 transmembrane domains)
  • Endogenous ligand: Ghrelin (pKd 8.6-9.4)
  • GHRP-6 binding affinity: pKd 6.6-8.8; pKi 8.8 (full agonist)
  • Constitutive activity: Yes - receptor maintains basal activity without ligand

Primary Signaling (IUPHAR):

  • Transduction: Gq/G11-mediated phospholipase C stimulation

  • G protein-independent: Beta-arrestin signaling

  • May contribute to anti-fibrotic and cardioprotective actions

Supported Mechanisms (Human Evidence)

GH Release:

  • Stimulates GH synthesis and secretion from pituitary somatotrophs [PMID: 7772238]
  • Acts through protein kinase C (PKC) and Ca²⁺ mobilization—cAMP-independent pathway
  • Activates phosphatidylinositol (PI) turnover in pituitary cells
  • Requires endogenous GHRH for maximal effect—synergistic interaction [PMID: 9543138]

Hypothalamic Effects:

  • GHRP-6 main action is exerted at hypothalamic level [PMID: 7883854]
  • Patients with hypothalamopituitary disconnection show blocked GHRP-6 response
  • Synergism with GHRH demonstrated in humans (1990, Bowers)

Non-Selective Effects (distinguishes from ipamorelin):

  • Increases ACTH and cortisol secretion [PMID: 9285939]
  • Increases prolactin release (dose-dependent)
  • Strong appetite stimulation via hypothalamic circuits

Physiologic Context: The Ghrelin System

Native Ghrelin Physiology

Ghrelin is the endogenous ligand for the receptor that GHRP-6 activates. Understanding native ghrelin helps contextualize GHRP-6’s effects.

Endogenous ghrelin:

  • 28-amino acid peptide produced primarily by stomach fundus
  • Unique octanoyl modification on Ser3 required for GHS-R1a activation
  • Circulating levels rise during fasting, fall after meals
  • Primary “hunger hormone”—stimulates food intake and GH secretion

Normal physiologic functions:

What Happens with Exogenous GHS-R1a Agonism

Acute effects (single dose):

  • Rapid GH release (peak 15-30 minutes)
  • Transient cortisol and ACTH increase
  • Appetite stimulation
  • Mild flushing in some subjects

Chronic effects (theoretical/limited data):

  • Unknown whether tachyphylaxis develops
  • Potential for sustained appetite increase
  • Effects on GH pulsatility with repeated dosing unclear
  • No long-term human data available

  4. Hyperprolactinemia or prolactin-sensitive conditions

Inferences Section

This section contains logical reasoning from known evidence. These are NOT proven facts.

Inference 1: GHRP-6 may have clinical utility in myocardial protection

(a) Statement: GHRP-6 could reduce damage from myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury in humans.

(b) Why someone might believe it:

  • Robust preclinical data showing reduced infarct size
  • Multiple mechanisms identified (antioxidant, anti-apoptotic, mitochondrial preservation)
  • CD36 receptor provides GH-independent cardioprotective pathway
  • Safe in acute dosing based on human PK studies

(c) Supporting evidence:

  • Animal I/R models consistently show benefit [PMID: 28270686]
  • Doxorubicin cardiotoxicity model shows protection [PMID: 39267656]
  • Mechanistic studies show Bcl-2 upregulation, mitochondrial integrity
  • Broad safety profile in healthy volunteers supports feasibility

(d) Contradicting evidence:

  • No human efficacy data for cardiac endpoints
  • Many preclinical cardioprotection findings fail to translate
  • Timing and dosing for acute coronary syndromes unknown
  • Cortisol elevation could theoretically be harmful in acute stress

(e) Confidence: LOW

(f) Study needed: Phase 2 RCT in STEMI patients with infarct size (CMR) as primary endpoint.

Inference 2: Ipamorelin is superior to GHRP-6 for most clinical applications

(a) Statement: If a GH secretagogue were to be developed clinically, ipamorelin would be preferred over GHRP-6 due to superior selectivity.

(b) Why someone might believe it:

  • Ipamorelin has no effect on cortisol or prolactin at therapeutic doses
  • Similar GH-releasing potency to GHRP-6
  • Less appetite stimulation (could be advantage or disadvantage)
  • “Cleaner” pharmacologic profile

(c) Supporting evidence:

  • Head-to-head comparisons show equivalent GH release
  • Ipamorelin approved (then discontinued) for post-surgical ileus
  • Selectivity data consistent across studies [PMID: 9285939]

(d) Contradicting evidence:

  • GHRP-6’s non-selectivity may be advantageous for cytoprotection (CD36 effects)
  • Appetite stimulation could benefit cachexia patients
  • GHRP-6 has more research history and better characterized safety
  • Neither has been developed to approval for GH-related indications

(e) Confidence: MODERATE

(f) Study needed: Head-to-head comparison in relevant clinical population.

Inference 3: GHRP-6’s cytoprotective effects may be partially GH-independent

(a) Statement: Some of GHRP-6’s tissue-protective effects occur through GHS-R1a and CD36 signaling independent of GH release.

(b) Why someone might believe it:

  • CD36 is expressed on cardiomyocytes and mediates direct protection
  • Timing of protection (minutes) precedes GH effects
  • Some effects persist in GH receptor knockout models
  • Mechanistic studies show direct cellular signaling

(c) Supporting evidence:

  • CD36 binding demonstrated for GHRPs [PMID: 28270686]
  • Direct mitochondrial effects in isolated cardiomyocytes
  • EGF+GHRP-6 synergism suggests non-GH mechanisms

(d) Contradicting evidence:

  • Difficult to fully separate GH-dependent vs independent effects in vivo
  • CD36 affinity may be lower than GHS-R1a
  • Clinical relevance of GH-independent effects unknown

(e) Confidence: LOW-MODERATE (preclinical data supportive)

(f) Study needed: Studies with selective GH receptor antagonists to isolate GH-independent effects.

WADA Status

GHRP-6 is PROHIBITED under the World Anti-Doping Agency code.

  • Listed under S2: Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics
  • Specifically: “Growth Hormone Secretagogues (GHS)”
  • Banned both in-competition and out-of-competition
  • Detection methods available via mass spectrometry

Regulatory Considerations

Why GHRP-6 was never developed to approval:

  1. Less selective than subsequent compounds (ipamorelin)
  2. Cortisol/prolactin effects considered undesirable
  3. Industry shifted focus to non-peptide oral GHSs (MK-677)
  4. Competing GHRH analogs (tesamorelin) reached market first
  5. Limited IP protection for early peptide sequences

Research compound availability:

  • Available from research chemical suppliers
  • Quality/purity not assured outside pharmaceutical grade
  • Not manufactured to GMP standards
  • Legal status varies by jurisdiction
