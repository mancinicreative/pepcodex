Kisspeptin

Also known as: KISS1, Metastin, Kisspeptin-54, Kisspeptin-10, KP-54, KP-10

An endogenous neuropeptide that serves as the master regulator of reproductive function. Acts upstream of GnRH to control puberty, fertility, and the menstrual cycle. Under clinical investigation as a safer IVF trigger and treatment for hypothalamic amenorrhea. Pioneering research led by Imperial College London.

Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Master regulator of reproduction - controls GnRH release and the entire reproductive axis
  • Essential for puberty - genetic mutations cause failure to enter puberty
  • Phase 2 trials completed for IVF trigger and hypothalamic amenorrhea
  • Eliminates OHSS risk - major safety advantage over hCG in IVF
  • Modulates brain activity - affects sexual desire and emotional processing
  • Imperial College London leads global clinical research program

Evidence Strength: Moderate-High - Extensive human physiological studies; multiple Phase 2 clinical trials completed with positive results; no regulatory approvals yet.

Naming History

The name “KiSS-1” was coined in 1996 at Penn State University, named after Hershey’s Kisses chocolates (Hershey, Pennsylvania). Originally discovered as a metastasis suppressor gene in melanoma cells - hence the alternate name “metastin.” Its critical role in reproduction was discovered separately in 2003.

Development Timeline

  • 1996: KiSS-1 gene cloned as metastasis suppressor (Penn State)
  • 2001: Metastin identified as GPR54 ligand (Takeda Pharmaceutical, Japan)
  • 2003: Landmark discovery: KISS1R mutations cause hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (Harvard & INSERM)
  • 2005: First human study shows potent LH stimulation (Imperial College London)
  • 2014: First IVF trigger trial (Imperial College London)
  • 2017: Phase 2b IVF trial published in Lancet Diabetes Endocrinology
  • Present: Ongoing clinical development; no approvals yet

Mechanism of Action

How Kisspeptin Works

Kisspeptin is the master switch of reproduction. It sits at the very top of the hormonal cascade that controls fertility, puberty, and the menstrual cycle. Without kisspeptin signaling, the entire reproductive system shuts down.

Think of the reproductive system like a waterfall: Kisspeptin releases GnRH, which releases LH and FSH, which then act on the ovaries or testes to produce sex hormones. Kisspeptin controls the source of the waterfall. This is why mutations in the kisspeptin receptor completely prevent puberty.

What makes kisspeptin especially interesting for medicine is that it acts as a gentle physiological trigger rather than an overwhelming stimulus. Unlike synthetic GnRH analogs that can desensitize the system, kisspeptin works more naturally.

KNDy Neuron System

The arcuate kisspeptin neurons are called KNDy neurons because they co-express:

  • Kisspeptin - stimulates GnRH
  • Neurokinin B - stimulates kisspeptin release
  • Dynorphin - inhibits kisspeptin release

This creates an auto-regulatory system that generates the pulsatile GnRH rhythm essential for fertility.

Key Human Trials

IVF Trigger Trials (Imperial College London)

The Problem: Standard IVF uses hCG to trigger final egg maturation, but hCG carries significant risk of Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS) - a potentially life-threatening complication.

The Solution: Kisspeptin triggers a natural, short-lived LH surge that matures eggs without the prolonged ovarian stimulation that causes OHSS.

Phase 2b Trial (Lancet Diabetes Endocrinology 2017)

ParameterDetails
DesignRandomized controlled trial
PopulationWomen at high risk of OHSS undergoing IVF
InterventionKisspeptin-54 as trigger vs standard hCG
Primary outcomeOocyte maturation rate

Key Results:

  • Comparable oocyte maturation rates to hCG
  • Zero cases of OHSS in kisspeptin group
  • Successful pregnancies achieved
  • Well tolerated

Hypothalamic Amenorrhea Studies

The Problem: Women with hypothalamic amenorrhea (HA) - often caused by stress, weight loss, or excessive exercise - have suppressed GnRH pulsatility and cannot ovulate.

The Discovery: Kisspeptin can restart the GnRH pulse generator in these women.

Path to Approval

Kisspeptin faces several development challenges:

  • Natural peptide - complex manufacturing and stability
  • Short half-life - may require analog development
  • Novel mechanism - requires regulatory education
  • Academic development - limited commercial investment

Beyond Fertility

Emerging research suggests kisspeptin may have roles in:
