faqs:
  - question: What is Retinalamin?
    answer: >-
      Retinalamin is a peptide complex extracted from bovine or porcine retinal
      tissue, developed by Russian scientist Vladimir Khavinson. It contains
      multiple low-molecular-weight peptides and is used in Russia for various
      retinal conditions including macular degeneration and diabetic
      retinopathy.
  - question: Is Retinalamin FDA approved?
    answer: >-
      No, Retinalamin is not approved by the FDA or any Western regulatory
      agency. It is registered as a pharmaceutical drug only in Russia and some
      CIS countries for ophthalmologic use.
  - question: What is the evidence for Retinalamin?
    answer: >-
      Evidence is low quality by Western standards. Most studies are Russian,
      open-label, and lack placebo controls or adequate blinding. While Russian
      clinical experience is extensive, no independent Western clinical trials
      have validated the claimed benefits.
  - question: How is Retinalamin administered?
    answer: >-
      In Russian clinical practice, retinalamin is administered via parabulbar
      injection (around the eye) or intramuscular injection. Typical courses
      involve 5-10 daily injections, repeated every 6-12 months. This is a
      medical procedure requiring trained personnel.
  - question: Is Retinalamin safe?
    answer: >-
      Russian clinical experience reports low incidence of adverse effects,
      primarily local injection site reactions. However, formal safety data from
      controlled trials is lacking, and risks of tissue-derived peptide products
      (immunogenicity, contamination) have not been rigorously evaluated by
      Western standards.
  - question: Can Retinalamin cure macular degeneration?
    answer: >-
      There is no evidence that retinalamin cures macular degeneration. Russian
      studies claim stabilization or modest improvement of visual function in
      some patients, but these findings have not been replicated in controlled
      Western trials. It is not considered a standard treatment for AMD
      internationally.

**Key Research Context:** All mechanistic research originates from Russian institutes. The exact composition of retinalamin varies by batch as it is a tissue extract, making precise molecular characterization impossible. This is a fundamental limitation compared to synthetic peptides with defined sequences.

### Important Limitations

- **Undefined composition:** Not a single molecule but a peptide mixture; exact active components unknown
- **No Western validation:** All clinical studies are Russian; no independent replication
- **Study quality issues:** Most trials are open-label without placebo controls or adequate blinding
- **Tissue-derived concerns:** Animal tissue extracts carry theoretical risks of contamination and immunogenicity
- **Administration barriers:** Requires injection by trained medical personnel
- **Regulatory status:** Not approved outside Russia/CIS; considered experimental elsewhere
- **Mechanism uncertainty:** Without defined sequence, molecular mechanisms remain speculative
