Melanotan II

Also known as: MT-II, MT-2

A non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist known for tanning effects. Not approved by any regulatory agency; associated with significant safety concerns including melanoma risk and systemic toxicity.

Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist affecting multiple MC receptors
  • Not FDA approved or approved by any major regulatory agency
  • Known for tanning effects through MC1R stimulation of melanocytes
  • Significant safety concerns including effects on moles and potential cancer risk
  • Subject to regulatory warnings in multiple countries

Evidence Strength: Low — Some human pharmacology data exists, but no Phase 3 trials. Regulatory agencies have issued warnings against use.

Important Warning

Regulatory agencies including the FDA, TGA (Australia), and EMA have issued warnings about Melanotan II. It is sold illegally and poses significant health risks.

Mechanism of Action

How Melanotan-II Works

Melanotan-II is essentially a “multi-target” drug that activates several different receptor types throughout your body simultaneously. This is both its appeal and its problem:

  1. Tanning without sun - MT-II tells skin cells (melanocytes) to produce more melanin, the pigment that darkens skin. Normally, your body only produces melanin after UV exposure damages your DNA. MT-II bypasses this safety mechanism, producing a tan without the UV trigger.

  2. Sexual arousal - MT-II crosses into the brain and activates receptors in areas that control sexual desire and arousal, similar to the approved drug PT-141. It can cause erections without any sexual stimulation.

  3. Appetite suppression - By activating MC4R receptors in the hypothalamus, MT-II can reduce hunger.

Why this is dangerous: Your body uses UV-triggered tanning as a protective response. The DNA damage is the signal that says “we need more melanin protection.” MT-II causes tanning without this signal, potentially allowing skin changes and mole growth without the natural surveillance mechanisms. Multiple case reports have linked MT-II use to melanoma.

Receptor Binding Profile

MT-II is a cyclic heptapeptide analog of α-MSH with enhanced stability (half-life ~1-2 hours) and brain permeability. Unlike the approved drug PT-141 (which is more selective), MT-II binds to all melanocortin receptors except MC2R:

Tanning Pathway: MC1R Signaling

When MT-II binds to MC1R receptors on skin cells called melanocytes, it triggers melanin production:

MT-II binds MC1R on melanocytes
    ↓
Gαs activation → Adenylyl cyclase
    ↓
↑ cAMP → PKA activation
    ↓
MITF (Microphthalmia TF) activation
    ↓
Tyrosinase gene transcription ↑
    ↓
Tyrosine → DOPA → Dopaquinone
    ↓
├── Eumelanin (brown-black) ↑ (the "good" melanin)
└── Pheomelanin (red-yellow) ↓ (associated with sun damage)

The critical concern: Tanning occurs without UV exposure. Normally, UV damage to DNA is the trigger that tells skin to produce protective melanin. By bypassing this, MT-II may promote pigment changes in moles and skin without the body’s normal cancer-surveillance mechanisms being activated.

Sexual Function Pathway: MC3R/MC4R Signaling

MT-II also affects the brain, triggering arousal pathways:

MT-II crosses blood-brain barrier
    ↓
Binds MC4R in medial preoptic area (mPOA)
    ↓
↑ Dopamine release in mPOA
    ↓
├── Erections without sexual stimulation
├── ↑ Sexual desire and arousal
└── ↑ Copulatory behavior

MT-II can trigger erections without any sexual stimulation — a sign of direct brain activation rather than normal arousal pathways.

Appetite Suppression: MC4R

MC4R activation in the hypothalamus suppresses appetite:

  • Reduces food intake even when fasting
  • Effect blocked by MC3R/MC4R antagonists
  • Can cause pronounced loss of appetite

Why MT-II Was Never Approved

Regulatory agencies rejected MT-II for these reasons:

  1. Non-selectivity - Affects too many systems at once, causing unpredictable effects
  2. Mole changes - Darkening and changes to existing moles, potentially masking or promoting melanoma
  3. Cardiovascular effects - Increases blood pressure (pressor effects)
  4. Theoretical cancer risk - Uncontrolled melanin production without normal DNA-damage surveillance
  5. Better alternative exists - PT-141 was developed as a more selective option for sexual function
