<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

## Quick Summary

- **Multi-receptor somatostatin analog** targeting SSTR1, SSTR2, SSTR3, and SSTR5 with broader receptor coverage than first-generation analogs
- **FDA approved** as Signifor (subcutaneous, 2012) for Cushing's disease and Signifor LAR (intramuscular, 2014) for acromegaly
- **Superior receptor affinity:** 30-fold higher affinity for SSTR1 and 39-fold higher for SSTR5 compared to octreotide
- **PAOLA study demonstrated superiority** to octreotide LAR in biochemically uncontrolled acromegaly (31.3% vs 19.2% GH/IGF-1 control)
- **Unique efficacy in resistant disease:** Effective in patients inadequately controlled on first-generation somatostatin analogs
- **Notable metabolic effects:** Hyperglycemia is the most clinically significant adverse effect, requiring proactive glucose monitoring

**Evidence Strength: HIGH** — Supported by multiple Phase 3 RCTs (PASPORT, PAOLA, CSOM230B2412), regulatory approvals in multiple jurisdictions, and well-characterized safety profile with over a decade of clinical use.

### Why Pasireotide Was Developed

First-generation somatostatin analogs (octreotide, lanreotide) bind primarily to SSTR2, with limited affinity for other receptor subtypes. However:

1. **Receptor heterogeneity:** Pituitary adenomas express variable SSTR profiles; many have high SSTR5 expression
2. **Treatment resistance:** 25-50% of acromegaly patients are inadequately controlled on octreotide/lanreotide
3. **Cushing's disease unmet need:** No FDA-approved medical therapy existed for Cushing's disease prior to 2012
4. **Corticotroph adenomas:** Express predominantly SSTR5 rather than SSTR2 [PMID: 15315000]

**Design rationale:** Pasireotide was engineered as a cyclic hexapeptide with high affinity across multiple SSTR subtypes to address these limitations.

<h2 id="mechanism-of-action">Mechanism of Action</h2>

### How Pasireotide Works (Plain English)

Pasireotide mimics somatostatin, a natural hormone that acts as a "brake" on many glandular secretions. Here's how it works:

1. **Broader receptor targeting** — Your body has five types of somatostatin receptors (SSTR1-5). Older drugs like octreotide mainly work through one type (SSTR2). Pasireotide works through four types (SSTR1, 2, 3, and 5), like having four keys instead of one.

2. **Controls excess cortisol in Cushing's disease** — In Cushing's disease, a pituitary tumor makes too much ACTH, which causes your adrenal glands to produce excess cortisol. Pasireotide directly suppresses these tumor cells, reducing ACTH and therefore cortisol. The tumor cells have SSTR5 receptors that pasireotide targets effectively.

3. **Controls growth hormone in acromegaly** — In acromegaly, a pituitary tumor produces too much growth hormone (GH). Pasireotide suppresses both GH secretion and tumor cell proliferation. Because it works through multiple receptors, it can help patients who don't respond to octreotide.

4. **Reduces IGF-1 levels** — Growth hormone stimulates IGF-1 production in the liver. By reducing GH, pasireotide lowers IGF-1, which drives many acromegaly symptoms.

5. **Important metabolic effect** — Pasireotide also reduces insulin secretion (through SSTR5 on pancreatic beta cells), which can raise blood sugar. This is why glucose monitoring is important during treatment.

**Why multiple receptors matter:** Tumors vary in which receptors they express. By targeting four receptor types, pasireotide works in more patients, including those who failed on single-receptor drugs.

### Receptor Target Profile: Multi-SSTR Agonist (Scientific Detail)

Pasireotide is a **pan-somatostatin receptor agonist** with highest affinity for SSTR5, followed by SSTR2, SSTR3, and SSTR1.

**Receptor Binding Affinity (IC50, nM) — Comparative:**

| Receptor | Pasireotide | Octreotide | Lanreotide | Native Somatostatin-14 | Relative Pasireotide Advantage |
|----------|-------------|------------|------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **SSTR1** | 9.3 | &gt;1,000 | &gt;1,000 | 2.3 | **30-fold vs octreotide** |
| **SSTR2** | 1.0 | 0.4 | 0.5 | 0.2 | ~0.4x (octreotide slightly higher) |
| **SSTR3** | 1.5 | 34 | 14 | 1.1 | ~23-fold vs octreotide |
| **SSTR4** | &gt;100 | &gt;1,000 | 230 | 1.8 | Minimal activity (both) |
| **SSTR5** | 0.16 | 7 | 17 | 0.9 | **39-fold vs octreotide** |

*Data from [PMID: 12200368] and [PMID: 22238397]*

**Key insight:** The dramatically higher SSTR5 affinity is crucial for Cushing's disease, where corticotroph adenomas preferentially express SSTR5.

### SSTR Expression and Functional Effects

| Tissue | Dominant SSTR | Pasireotide Effect | Clinical Implication |
|--------|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Corticotroph adenomas** | SSTR5 >> SSTR2 | Suppresses ACTH secretion | Treats Cushing's disease |
| **GH-secreting adenomas** | SSTR2 > SSTR5 | Suppresses GH secretion | Treats acromegaly |
| **Pancreatic beta cells** | SSTR5 > SSTR2 | Inhibits insulin secretion | Causes hyperglycemia |
| **Gut endocrine cells** | SSTR2, SSTR5 | Inhibits GI hormone release | GI side effects |
| **NETs** | Variable (SSTR2, SSTR5) | Tumor control, symptom relief | Potential indication |

### Signaling Pathway Overview

**Primary pathway:**
```
Pasireotide binds SSTR1/2/3/5
    |
Gi/Go protein activation
    |
+-- Adenylyl cyclase inhibition --> ↓cAMP --> ↓PKA
|
+-- K+ channel activation --> Membrane hyperpolarization
|
+-- Ca2+ channel inhibition --> ↓Ca2+ influx
|
+-- MAPK pathway modulation --> Antiproliferative effects
    |
    ↓
+-- Pituitary: ↓GH, ↓ACTH secretion
+-- Pancreas: ↓Insulin, ↓Glucagon secretion
+-- GI tract: ↓Motility, ↓Secretion
```

**In simpler terms:** When pasireotide binds to somatostatin receptors, it activates "stop" signals inside cells. These signals reduce both hormone secretion (like GH and ACTH) and cell growth. The same mechanism in pancreatic cells reduces insulin release, explaining why blood sugar rises during treatment.

### Supported Mechanisms (Direct Human Evidence)

**Cushing's disease:**
- Direct suppression of ACTH secretion from corticotroph adenomas [PMID: 22990096]
- Consequent reduction in cortisol production
- Normalization of urinary free cortisol (UFC) in responders

**Acromegaly:**
- Suppression of GH secretion from somatotroph adenomas [PMID: 28253614]
- Secondary reduction in hepatic IGF-1 production
- Partial tumor shrinkage in some patients

**Hyperglycemia mechanism:**
- Direct inhibition of insulin secretion via SSTR5 on beta cells [PMID: 23345097]
- Reduced incretin effect (GLP-1, GIP suppression)
- Modest suppression of glucagon (net hyperglycemic effect)

<h2 id="key-human-trials">Key Human Trials</h2>

### Cushing's Disease Trials

#### CSOM230B2305 (Phase 3 Pivotal Trial)

**Trial design:** Open-label, multicenter study of pasireotide SC in adults with Cushing's disease [PMID: 22990096]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **N** | 162 |
| **Population** | Adults with Cushing's disease, persistent/recurrent after surgery or not surgical candidates |
| **Doses** | 600 mcg or 900 mcg SC twice daily |
| **Duration** | 12 months |
| **Primary endpoint** | UFC normalization at 6 months without dose increase |
| **Key result** | **26.3% (600 mcg) and 14.6% (900 mcg) achieved UFC normalization** |
| **NCT ID** | [NCT00434148](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT00434148) |

**Detailed results:**
- Mean UFC reduction: 47.9% from baseline
- Partial response (UFC decreased ≥50% but not normalized): additional 25-30%
- Clinical improvements: reduced blood pressure, weight, and LDL cholesterol
- Tumor shrinkage: 9.1% had ≥20% tumor volume reduction

**Long-term extension data:**
- Sustained efficacy at 24 months in responders
- Some patients maintained control for &gt;5 years

### Acromegaly Trials

#### PAOLA Study (Phase 3, Head-to-Head)

**Trial design:** Randomized, double-blind, active-controlled superiority trial comparing pasireotide LAR to octreotide LAR in patients with inadequately controlled acromegaly [PMID: 28253614]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **N** | 358 |
| **Population** | Acromegaly inadequately controlled on first-generation SSAs |
| **Arms** | Pasireotide LAR 40mg or 60mg vs Octreotide LAR 30mg (monthly IM) |
| **Duration** | 24 weeks (core) + extension |
| **Primary endpoint** | GH &lt;2.5 μg/L AND IGF-1 normalization at 24 weeks |
| **NCT ID** | [NCT01137682](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT01137682) |

**Key results:**

| Outcome | Pasireotide LAR 40mg | Pasireotide LAR 60mg | Octreotide LAR 30mg |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Primary endpoint** | 15.4% | **20.0%** | 0% |
| **GH &lt;2.5 μg/L** | 35.8% | 48.3% | 28.6% |
| **IGF-1 normalization** | 19.2% | 27.5% | 8.2% |
| **Biochemical control (per protocol)** | 31.3% | — | 19.2% |

**Superiority confirmed:** p&lt;0.001 for both pasireotide doses vs octreotide

#### CSOM230C2305 (Treatment-Naive Acromegaly)

**Trial design:** Randomized, blinded, active-controlled trial in drug-naive acromegaly patients [PMID: 24423324]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **N** | 358 |
| **Population** | Acromegaly, previously untreated with SSAs |
| **Arms** | Pasireotide LAR 40mg vs Octreotide LAR 20mg (monthly IM) |
| **Duration** | 12 months |
| **Primary endpoint** | GH &lt;2.5 μg/L AND IGF-1 normalization at 12 months |
| **NCT ID** | [NCT00600886](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT00600886) |

**Key results:**

| Outcome | Pasireotide LAR | Octreotide LAR |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Primary endpoint** | **31.3%** | **19.2%** |
| **Difference** | +12.1% (p=0.007) |
| **GH &lt;2.5 μg/L** | 38.6% | 23.6% |
| **IGF-1 normalization** | 38.6% | 23.0% |
| **Tumor volume reduction &gt;20%** | 80.8% | 77.4% |

**Key finding:** Pasireotide LAR was superior to octreotide LAR as first-line medical therapy for acromegaly.

#### CSOM230B2412 (Long-Acting Crossover Study)

**Trial design:** Open-label extension study evaluating long-term pasireotide LAR in patients who crossed over from octreotide/lanreotide [PMID: 25337926]

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **N** | 61 (crossover population) |
| **Population** | Acromegaly inadequately controlled on first-generation SSAs |
| **Endpoint** | Biochemical control after switching to pasireotide LAR |
| **Duration** | 24 months |

**Results:**
- 17.3% achieved biochemical control after switching from octreotide LAR
- 20.5% achieved biochemical control after switching from lanreotide Autogel
- Demonstrates "escape" option for SSA-resistant patients

### Neuroendocrine Tumor Data

**CSOM230C2110 (Phase 2):**
- Evaluated pasireotide LAR in metastatic NETs progressing on octreotide LAR
- Showed antiproliferative activity in some patients
- Not FDA-approved for this indication but studied in clinical trials [PMID: 25667288]

| **Headache** | 5-10% | Mild | Variable |
| **Fatigue** | 5-10% | Mild | Variable |

### Hyperglycemia: The Critical Safety Consideration

**Incidence and severity:**
- New-onset hyperglycemia: 40-50% of patients
- Diabetes mellitus diagnosis: 20-30% of previously normoglycemic patients
- HbA1c increase: Mean +1-2% in clinical trials
- FPG increase: Mean +30-50 mg/dL

**Mechanism:** Direct SSTR5-mediated inhibition of insulin secretion, with additional suppression of incretin hormones.

**Risk factors for severe hyperglycemia:**
- Pre-existing glucose intolerance
- Higher baseline HbA1c
- Higher pasireotide dose
- Concurrent corticosteroid therapy (Cushing's disease patients)

**Management approach:**
1. **Pre-treatment:** FPG, HbA1c assessment; diabetes risk evaluation
2. **Week 1-4:** Weekly FPG monitoring
3. **Ongoing:** Monthly FPG; HbA1c every 3 months
4. **Treatment:** Metformin first-line; DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists, or insulin as needed
5. **Dose adjustment:** Consider dose reduction if hyperglycemia severe and uncontrolled

**Important note:** In Cushing's disease, glucose metabolism may initially worsen (due to pasireotide) then improve as cortisol normalizes. Net glycemic effect varies.

### Serious Adverse Events and Warnings

| Risk | Evidence Type | Frequency | Current Understanding |
|------|---------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Hyperglycemia/DM** | Phase 3 RCTs | 40-70% | **Boxed warning (in some regions)**; proactive monitoring required |
| **Cholelithiasis** | Phase 3 RCTs | 15-30% | Class effect of SSAs; ultrasound monitoring recommended |
| **Bradycardia** | Phase 3 RCTs | 5-10% | Usually asymptomatic; ECG monitoring at baseline |
| **QT prolongation** | Phase 3 RCTs | Rare | Caution with QT-prolonging drugs |
| **Hepatic enzyme elevation** | Phase 3 RCTs | 5-10% | Monitor LFTs; usually transient |
| **Adrenal insufficiency** | Cushing's trials | Rare | Possible with rapid cortisol lowering |
| **Hypothyroidism** | Phase 3 RCTs | &lt;5% | Monitor TSH periodically |

### Contraindications

1. **Known hypersensitivity to pasireotide or excipients**
2. **Severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh C)**

### Precautions

1. **Diabetes or glucose intolerance** — Requires intensified monitoring and management
2. **Cardiac conduction disorders** — Baseline ECG; avoid with significant QT prolongation
3. **Cholelithiasis history** — Ultrasound monitoring recommended
4. **Hepatic impairment** — Dose adjustment in moderate impairment; avoid in severe

### Special Populations

| Population | Recommendation | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-------|
| **Pregnancy** | Not recommended | Limited data; potential fetal harm |
| **Breastfeeding** | Not recommended | Unknown excretion in milk |
| **Renal impairment** | No dose adjustment | PK not significantly altered |
| **Hepatic impairment** | Reduce dose (moderate) | Avoid in severe impairment |
| **Elderly** | Use with caution | Higher hyperglycemia risk |

**Cushing's disease (SC formulation):**
- Starting dose: 600 mcg or 900 mcg SC twice daily
- Titration: Based on UFC response and tolerability
- Maximum: 900 mcg twice daily

**Acromegaly (LAR formulation):**
- Starting dose: 40 mg IM every 28 days
- Titration: May increase to 60 mg based on response
- Maximum: 60 mg every 28 days

<h2 id="comparisons">Comparisons</h2>

### Pasireotide vs Octreotide

| Factor | Pasireotide | Octreotide |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Mechanism** | Multi-SSTR agonist (1,2,3,5) | Primarily SSTR2 agonist |
| **SSTR5 affinity** | IC50 = 0.16 nM | IC50 = 7 nM (39x lower) |
| **SSTR1 affinity** | IC50 = 9.3 nM | IC50 &gt;1,000 nM (&gt;100x lower) |
| **Cushing's disease** | **FDA approved** | Not approved |
| **Acromegaly control (naive)** | 31.3% (PAOLA) | 19.2% (p=0.007) |
| **Acromegaly (resistant)** | Effective | Often fails |
| **Hyperglycemia** | 40-70% (more common) | 10-20% (less common) |
| **Cost** | Higher | Lower (generic available) |
| **Years on market** | Since 2012 | Since 1988 |

**Head-to-head summary:** PAOLA trial demonstrated pasireotide LAR superiority to octreotide LAR in both treatment-naive and SSA-resistant acromegaly. The key trade-off is greater hyperglycemia risk with pasireotide.

### Pasireotide vs Lanreotide

| Factor | Pasireotide | Lanreotide |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Mechanism** | Multi-SSTR agonist (1,2,3,5) | Primarily SSTR2 agonist |
| **SSTR5 affinity** | Very high | Low |
| **Cushing's disease** | **FDA approved** | Not approved |
| **Acromegaly efficacy** | Higher in resistant patients | Similar to octreotide |
| **Hyperglycemia** | More common | Less common |
| **GI tolerability** | Similar | Similar |

### When to Choose Pasireotide

**Consider pasireotide when:**
1. Patient has Cushing's disease (medical therapy indicated)
2. Acromegaly inadequately controlled on octreotide/lanreotide
3. Tumor likely high SSTR5 expression (e.g., corticotroph adenoma)
4. Second-line or third-line medical therapy needed

**Consider first-generation SSA (octreotide/lanreotide) when:**
1. Treatment-naive acromegaly (unless contraindicated)
2. Patient has pre-existing diabetes with poor control
3. Cost is a significant consideration
4. Lower metabolic risk profile preferred

<h2 id="editorial-notes">Editorial Notes</h2>

**Date generated:** January 2026

**Last updated:** January 22, 2026

**Sources consulted:** PubMed, Europe PMC, ClinicalTrials.gov, FDA Drugs@FDA, EMA EPAR database, Novartis prescribing information

**Key clinical considerations:**

1. **Hyperglycemia management is essential** — Unlike first-generation SSAs, pasireotide reliably causes glucose elevations. Pre-treatment assessment and proactive monitoring/management are mandatory.

2. **Position in treatment algorithm** — Consider as first-line for Cushing's disease; consider as second-line (or first-line in selected cases) for acromegaly based on receptor profile or resistance to first-generation SSAs.

3. **Cost-benefit analysis** — Higher cost and metabolic burden balanced against efficacy in resistant disease.

**Update triggers that would require revision:**
- New indication approvals (e.g., NETs)
- Head-to-head trials vs newer agents (osilodrostat, levoketoconazole)
- Long-term outcome studies
- Any new FDA safety communication

**Limitations of this dossier:**
- Focused on approved indications only
- Does not cover off-label uses
- No dosing guidance provided per editorial policy
- Limited pediatric data
