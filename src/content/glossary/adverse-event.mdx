## Terminology Distinctions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Adverse Event (AE) | Any bad outcome while on treatment (may or may not be drug-related) |
| Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR) | Event where drug causation is suspected |
| Side Effect | Expected pharmacological effect (can be wanted or unwanted) |
| Serious Adverse Event (SAE) | AE that is life-threatening, requires hospitalization, or causes disability |

## Adverse Event Classification

### By Severity

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Mild | Noticeable but no interference with activities |
| Moderate | Interferes with some activities |
| Severe | Prevents normal activities |
| Life-threatening | Immediate risk of death |
| Death | Fatal outcome |

### By Causality
- **Definite**: Clear temporal relationship, improves when stopped, recurs on rechallenge
- **Probable**: Reasonable temporal relationship, unlikely other cause
- **Possible**: Could be drug or could be other factors
- **Unlikely**: Probably not drug-related
- **Unrelated**: Clearly not drug-related

## Serious Adverse Events (SAEs)

### Definition
An SAE is any event that:
- Results in death
- Is life-threatening
- Requires hospitalization
- Results in disability
- Causes birth defect
- Is otherwise medically important

### GLP-1 Agonist SAEs Under Investigation
- Pancreatitis (rare)
- Gallbladder disease
- Thyroid tumors (animal studies; human risk uncertain)
- Suicidal ideation (under investigation)
- Gastroparesis (severe delayed emptying)

## Adverse Event Reporting

### During Clinical Trials
- All AEs documented
- Investigators assess causality
- Reported to sponsor and regulators
- Compiled for safety analysis

### Post-Marketing
- FAERS (FDA Adverse Event Reporting System)
- MedWatch
- Healthcare providers should report
- Patients can report directly

## Reading Adverse Event Data

### What Numbers Mean

| Metric | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Incidence | % of patients experiencing event |
| NNH | Number needed to harm (cause 1 event) |
| Relative risk | Compared to placebo |
| Attributable risk | Excess events due to drug |

### Example Interpretation
"Nausea occurred in 40% on drug vs 10% on placebo"
- Absolute difference: 30%
- Relative risk: 4x higher
- NNH: ~3-4 (1 extra nausea case per 3-4 treated)

## Managing Adverse Events

### General Strategies
| Strategy | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Slow titration | Start low, increase gradually |
| Dose reduction | If events intolerable |
| Symptomatic treatment | OTC remedies when appropriate |
| Watchful waiting | Many events improve with time |
| Discontinuation | If severe or persistent |

### GI Events Specifically
- Eat smaller meals
- Avoid fatty foods
- Stay hydrated
- Ginger for nausea
- Fiber for constipation
- Anti-nausea medication if needed

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does experiencing side effects mean the drug is working?

Not necessarily. Adverse events indicate drug activity in the body but don't correlate with efficacy. Some people have significant weight loss with minimal side effects; others have side effects without good response.

### Should I stop medication if I have side effects?

Depends on severity and type. Many GI side effects improve over weeks and can be managed. Severe or concerning symptoms (severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting) warrant medical evaluation. Don't stop suddenly without guidance.

### Are adverse events in trials representative of real-world use?

Somewhat. Trials have strict protocols and monitoring. Real-world use may see different patterns due to varied populations, less oversight, and different dosing behaviors. Post-marketing surveillance captures real-world safety data.
