<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

## Quick Summary

- **Porcine brain-derived peptide complex** extracted from pig cerebral cortex
- **Approved in Russia** and some CIS countries since 1999 for stroke, TBI, cognitive disorders
- **NOT approved** by FDA, EMA, MHRA, or other Western regulatory agencies
- **Manufacturer:** GEROPHARM (St. Petersburg, Russia)
- **Similar concept to Cerebrolysin** but derived from cortex rather than whole brain
- **Claimed uses:** Stroke recovery, cognitive enhancement, pediatric neurology, TBI
- **Evidence:** Extensive Russian literature, very limited Western validation

**Evidence Strength: LOW** — Regulatory approval and clinical use in Russia with multiple published studies, but minimal independent Western replication and most trials do not meet current Western methodological standards.

<h2 id="mechanism-of-action">Mechanism of Action</h2>

### How Cortexin Works (Plain English)

Cortexin contains small protein fragments derived from the outer layer of pig brain (the cortex - responsible for higher thinking). These fragments are claimed to:

1. **Provide neurotrophic support** - The peptides may mimic natural brain growth factors that help neurons survive and function properly.

2. **Protect neurons from damage** - In laboratory studies, cortexin extracts protect brain cells from various stressors like low oxygen or toxins.

3. **Modulate neurotransmitters** - Claimed to help balance brain chemistry, particularly dopamine and serotonin systems.

4. **Support brain metabolism** - Proposed to improve how brain cells use energy and oxygen.

**Important caveat:** Unlike Cerebrolysin, Cortexin has not undergone rigorous Western mechanistic study. Most mechanism claims come from Russian preclinical work that has not been independently replicated.

### Proposed Receptor Targets

| System | Proposed Effect | Evidence Level |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Neurotrophic factors** | BDNF-like, NGF-like activity | Low (Russian in vitro) |
| **Glutamate system** | Anti-excitotoxic effects | Low (animal studies) |
| **Dopaminergic** | Modulation | Very low |
| **GABAergic** | Modulation | Very low |
| **Antioxidant** | Free radical scavenging | Low (in vitro) |

### Signaling Pathway Overview (Proposed)

```
Cortexin peptides enter CNS (claimed to cross BBB)
    |
Multiple peptide-receptor interactions (poorly characterized)
    |
+-- Neurotrophic support -> Neuronal survival
|
+-- Anti-oxidant effects -> Reduced oxidative damage
```

**Key limitation:** The exact peptide composition and specific molecular targets remain poorly characterized. This is a significant weakness compared to defined-compound neuroprotective drugs.

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

Cortexin represents a Russian neuropeptide therapy with:

**What We Know:**
- Porcine cerebral cortex extract developed in Russia
- Approved and used clinically in Russia since 1999
- Claims neurotrophic and neuroprotective properties
- Similar concept to Cerebrolysin (better-studied Austrian product)

**What We Don't Know:**
- Exact active peptide composition
- Whether Russian efficacy claims translate to Western-standard trials
- Long-term safety by international standards
- Whether it offers advantages over Cerebrolysin

**Critical Limitations:**
- No Western regulatory approval
- Minimal independent research validation
- Most trials do not meet modern methodological standards
- Extract nature prevents precise characterization

**Bottom Line:** Cortexin has a history in Russian neurology but lacks the international validation of similar products like Cerebrolysin. For those interested in brain-derived neuropeptides, Cerebrolysin offers more robust evidence, though neither is FDA-approved. The low evidence rating reflects genuine uncertainty about efficacy rather than evidence of ineffectiveness.
