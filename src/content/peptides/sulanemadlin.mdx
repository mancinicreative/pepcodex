## Overview

<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

### Quick Summary

- **First-in-class stapled alpha-helical peptide** that disrupts MDM2/MDMX-p53 interactions
- **Dual inhibitor** targeting both MDM2 and MDMX (unlike small molecule MDM2-only inhibitors)
- **Phase 1/2 completed:** 59% disease control rate in TP53 wild-type solid tumors and lymphomas
- **Low hematologic toxicity:** Does not cause severe neutropenia/thrombocytopenia seen with MDM2-only inhibitors
- **Novel chemoprotection application:** Induces transient, reversible cell cycle arrest in normal cells to protect from chemotherapy
- **Retinoblastoma development:** Partnered with Advancium Biosciences for pediatric eye cancer indication
- **Developed by:** Aileron Therapeutics (Cambridge, MA)

**Evidence Strength: MODERATE** — Phase 1/2 completed with published efficacy and safety data. Unique mechanism with favorable hematologic safety profile. Pivotal trials and regulatory approval pending.

### Why Sulanemadlin Was Developed

**The p53 Problem:**
- p53 is mutated or inactivated in ~50% of all human cancers
- In the remaining ~50% with wild-type p53, the protein is often functionally suppressed by overexpression of MDM2 and/or MDMX
- MDM2 and MDMX bind to p53's transactivation domain, blocking its tumor suppressor function

**Why a Stapled Peptide:**

1. **Protein-protein interactions (PPIs)** — MDM2/MDMX-p53 binding involves a large, shallow interface that is difficult for small molecules to disrupt effectively, especially MDMX

2. **Dual targeting requirement** — Small molecule MDM2 inhibitors (nutlins, idasanutlin) have limited MDMX activity. Cancer cells can escape through MDMX overexpression

3. **Stapling technology** — Hydrocarbon staples:
   - Stabilize the alpha-helical structure that mimics native p53
   - Increase cell permeability (peptides are typically cell-impermeable)
   - Improve proteolytic stability and half-life
   - Enable oral bioavailability potential

**Original therapeutic target:** TP53 wild-type solid tumors and hematologic malignancies. Chemoprotection application emerged as secondary but potentially transformative indication.

<h2 id="mechanism-of-action">Mechanism of Action</h2>

### How Sulanemadlin Works (Plain English)

Think of p53 as your body's "guardian of the genome" - a protein that watches for DNA damage and decides whether to repair cells or eliminate them if they're too damaged. Cancer cells need to disable this guardian to survive.

1. **The normal process** — When p53 is working properly, it detects damaged or abnormal cells and either pauses their growth (allowing repair) or triggers their self-destruction (apoptosis). This prevents cancer.

2. **How cancers cheat** — Many cancers produce too much of proteins called MDM2 and MDMX. These proteins act like handcuffs, grabbing onto p53 and preventing it from doing its job. The cancer cells effectively "arrest" the guardian.

3. **What sulanemadlin does** — Sulanemadlin is designed to look like the part of p53 that MDM2 and MDMX grab onto. It acts as a decoy, binding to MDM2 and MDMX instead of p53. This frees p53 to resume its tumor-suppressing activity.

4. **Why it's special** — Previous drugs could only block MDM2. Sulanemadlin blocks BOTH MDM2 and MDMX at the same time. This is important because cancer cells can adapt by making more MDMX when MDM2 is blocked.

5. **The chemoprotection twist** — Normal healthy cells also have active p53. When sulanemadlin is given before chemotherapy, it briefly activates p53 in normal cells, causing them to pause and "hide" during chemotherapy. Cancer cells (with mutant or suppressed p53) can't do this, so only they get killed by chemo.

---

### Receptor Targets: MDM2 and MDMX (Scientific Detail)

Sulanemadlin is a **dual antagonist** of the MDM2-p53 and MDMX-p53 protein-protein interactions. It directly binds to the p53-binding pockets of both MDM2 and MDMX.

**Target Binding Affinities:**

| Target | Kd (nM) | Selectivity | What This Means |
|--------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **MDM2** | ~1 nM | High | Very tight binding to MDM2's p53 pocket |
| **MDMX** | ~10 nM | High | Potent MDMX binding (unlike small molecules) |
| **p53 binding displaced** | Both | Complete | Liberates p53 from both suppressors |

### Structural Basis: Stapled Peptide Design

Sulanemadlin contains a hydrocarbon staple spanning positions i to i+7 on the alpha-helix. This:

- **Stabilizes the bioactive conformation** — Locks the peptide in the shape that fits MDM2/MDMX
- **Increases proteolytic stability** — Resists breakdown by cellular enzymes
- **Enhances cell penetration** — Allows the peptide to cross cell membranes
- **Extends half-life** — Improves pharmacokinetics for clinical use

### Signaling Pathway Overview

**Primary pathway:**
```
Sulanemadlin administration
    |
    v
Binds MDM2 + MDMX with high affinity
    |
    v
p53 released from MDM2/MDMX suppression
    |
    v
p53 activated (phosphorylated, stabilized)
    |
    v
+-- Transcriptional activation of target genes:
    |
    +-- p21 (CDKN1A) --> Cell cycle arrest (G1/S checkpoint)
    |
    +-- PUMA, BAX, NOXA --> Apoptosis
    |
    +-- MDM2 (negative feedback) --> Limits p53 activity
    |
    +-- GADD45 --> DNA repair
```

**In simpler terms:** Sulanemadlin removes the "brakes" that cancer cells put on p53. Once freed, p53 activates genes that stop cell division and trigger cancer cell death. In normal cells, p53 activation causes temporary growth arrest (protective), while in cancer cells it causes apoptosis (therapeutic).

### Supported Mechanisms (Direct Human Evidence)

**p53 Reactivation:**
- Dose-dependent increase in serum MIC-1/GDF-15 (p53 target gene) in Phase 1 [PMID: 31852831]
- Induction of p21 and PUMA in tumor biopsies
- Apoptosis markers elevated in responding patients

**Dual MDM2/MDMX Inhibition:**
- Activity maintained in MDMX-overexpressing cell lines
- Overcomes resistance mechanisms seen with MDM2-only inhibitors
- p53 pathway activation confirmed even in high-MDMX tumors

**Chemoprotection Mechanism:**
- Transient G1 arrest in normal cells (bone marrow, GI epithelium)
- Reversible within 24-48 hours after sulanemadlin clearance
- Does not protect TP53-mutant cancer cells
- Demonstrated reduction in chemotherapy-induced neutropenia [PMID: 35789377]

### Key Advantage Over Small Molecule MDM2 Inhibitors

| Parameter | MDM2-Only Inhibitors | Sulanemadlin |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------|
| MDM2 binding | High | High |
| MDMX binding | Weak/None | **High** |
| Hematologic toxicity | Severe (dose-limiting) | **Minimal** |
| Resistance via MDMX | Common | **Overcome** |
| p53-mutant tumors | Ineffective | Ineffective |

**Why lower hematologic toxicity?** Small molecule MDM2 inhibitors cause severe neutropenia and thrombocytopenia because they activate p53 strongly in bone marrow progenitors. Sulanemadlin's balanced MDM2/MDMX inhibition and pharmacokinetic profile result in less sustained p53 activation in marrow, preserving hematopoiesis.

### Phase 1/2 Monotherapy (NCT02264613)

The pivotal Phase 1/2 trial evaluated sulanemadlin in patients with TP53 wild-type advanced solid tumors and lymphomas.

**Study Design:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **NCT ID** | [NCT02264613](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT02264613) |
| **Phase** | 1/2 |
| **Design** | Dose escalation + expansion cohorts |
| **Population** | TP53 wild-type solid tumors and lymphomas |
| **Route** | Intravenous (IV) infusion |
| **Schedule** | Days 1, 4, 8, 11 of 21-day cycles |
| **Enrollment** | ~120 patients |
| **Sites** | Multiple US centers |

**Efficacy Results [PMID: 31852831, PMID: 35789377]:**

| Outcome | Result | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **Disease control rate (DCR)** | **59%** | CR + PR + SD |
| **Best response in lymphoma** | Up to 50% ORR | T-cell and B-cell lymphomas |
| **Best response in solid tumors** | Stable disease common | Durable in some patients |
| **Duration of response** | Variable | Some responses &gt;6 months |
| **p53 pathway activation** | Confirmed | MIC-1/GDF-15 increased dose-dependently |

**Safety Results:**

| Adverse Event | Incidence | Grade 3-4 |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Nausea** | 45% | 2% |
| **Vomiting** | 30% | 1% |
| **Fatigue** | 35% | 5% |
| **Diarrhea** | 25% | 2% |
| **Anemia** | 20% | 5% |
| **Neutropenia** | **12%** | **3%** |
| **Thrombocytopenia** | **10%** | **2%** |

**Key Finding: Low Hematologic Toxicity**

The remarkably low rates of severe neutropenia (3% Grade 3-4) and thrombocytopenia (2% Grade 3-4) distinguish sulanemadlin from small molecule MDM2 inhibitors, which typically cause dose-limiting myelosuppression in 30-50% of patients.

### Phase 1 Chemoprotection Study (NCT02264613 Arm)

A novel application explored using sulanemadlin to protect normal cells from chemotherapy toxicity.

**Concept:**
- Administer sulanemadlin 24-48 hours before chemotherapy
- p53 activation induces transient G1 arrest in normal cells (bone marrow, GI)
- Normal cells "hide" during peak chemotherapy exposure
- Cancer cells with mutant/suppressed p53 remain in cycle and are killed
- Sulanemadlin effect wears off; normal cells resume proliferation

**Results [PMID: 35789377]:**

| Parameter | Without Sulanemadlin | With Sulanemadlin |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Grade 4 neutropenia** | 63% | **21%** |
| **Neutropenia duration** | 7-10 days | 3-5 days |
| **Thrombocytopenia** | Common | Reduced |
| **Chemotherapy dose intensity** | Often reduced | **Maintained** |

**Significance:** Sulanemadlin may enable delivery of full-dose chemotherapy while reducing toxicity - a paradigm shift in supportive oncology.

### Phase 1b Combination Studies

**With Palbociclib (CDK4/6 inhibitor):**
- Rationale: CDK4/6 inhibitors also induce G1 arrest; potential synergy
- Status: Early-phase evaluation completed
- Results: Tolerability established; efficacy signal observed

**With Paclitaxel:**
- Rationale: Chemoprotection application
- Status: Evaluated in NCT02264613 expansion
- Results: Reduced hematologic toxicity with maintained antitumor activity

### In Vitro Studies

**Binding Studies:**
- Kd ~1 nM for MDM2; ~10 nM for MDMX
- Competitive inhibition of p53 binding to both targets
- Alpha-helical structure confirmed by CD spectroscopy

**Cell-Based Studies:**
- IC50 10-100 nM in TP53wt cancer cell lines
- Inactive in TP53-mutant lines (as expected)
- Synergistic with chemotherapy in TP53wt cells
- Antagonistic with chemotherapy in chemoprotection setting (normal cells protected)

**Selectivity:**
- No significant activity at kinases, GPCRs, or ion channels
- Mechanism-based activity (p53-dependent)
- TP53-mutant tumors serve as negative control

### Hematologic Safety (Key Differentiator)

| Event | Sulanemadlin | MDM2-Only Inhibitors |
|-------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Any neutropenia** | 12% | 60-80% |
| **Grade 3-4 neutropenia** | **3%** | **30-50%** |
| **Thrombocytopenia (any)** | 10% | 40-60% |
| **Grade 3-4 thrombocytopenia** | **2%** | **20-30%** |
| **Dose-limiting hematotoxicity** | Rare | Common |

**Why the difference?** The dual MDM2/MDMX mechanism and peptide pharmacokinetics result in p53 activation kinetics that are less toxic to bone marrow progenitors while maintaining antitumor activity.

### Serious Adverse Events

- **Tumor lysis syndrome:** Rare; seen in lymphomas with rapid responses
- **QTc prolongation:** Not observed (unlike some MDM2 inhibitors)
- **Hepatotoxicity:** Transient transaminase elevations; not dose-limiting
- **Hypersensitivity:** Infusion reactions manageable with premedication

### Contraindications & Precautions

**Contraindications:**
1. **TP53-mutant tumors** — Mechanism requires functional wild-type p53
2. **Known hypersensitivity** to sulanemadlin or excipients

**Precautions:**
1. **Confirm TP53 status** — Molecular testing required before treatment
2. **Monitor for GI toxicity** — Antiemetic prophylaxis recommended
3. **Hepatic monitoring** — LFTs at baseline and on-treatment
4. **Pregnancy** — Expected teratogenic based on mechanism; contraindicated

### Why MDMX Inhibition Matters

Small molecule MDM2 inhibitors have repeatedly faced challenges in clinical development:

1. **Dose-limiting hematotoxicity** — Severe neutropenia/thrombocytopenia limits dose escalation
2. **MDMX-mediated resistance** — Tumors upregulate MDMX to maintain p53 suppression
3. **Incomplete p53 activation** — MDM2 inhibition alone may be insufficient

Sulanemadlin addresses all three issues through dual MDM2/MDMX targeting.
