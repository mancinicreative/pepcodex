## How Peptide Bonds Form

Peptide bond formation is a condensation reaction:

```
Amino Acid 1 + Amino Acid 2 → Dipeptide + Water
   (C-OH)      (H-N)            (C-N)      (H2O)
```

1. Carboxyl group (-COOH) of first amino acid
2. Amino group (-NH2) of second amino acid
3. Water molecule released
4. C-N bond formed (peptide bond)

## Peptide Bond Properties

| Property | Description | Importance |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| Planar | Atoms in same plane | Limits flexibility |
| Partial double bond | 40% double bond character | Restricts rotation |
| Trans configuration | Usually trans (more stable) | Affects folding |
| Resonance | Electrons delocalized | Stabilizes structure |

## Breaking Peptide Bonds

### Enzymatic Cleavage
- **Proteases/peptidases** break peptide bonds
- Specific enzymes target specific sequences
- This is why many peptides have short half-lives

### Chemical Hydrolysis
- Strong acid or base
- High temperature
- Returns amino acids

### Drug Design Implications
- Protecting peptide bonds from enzymes
- Using modified bonds (pseudopeptides)
- Strategic amino acid placement

## Peptide Bond Angles and Folding

The peptide bond restricts rotation around C-N:

| Angle | Name | Rotation |
|-------|------|----------|
| φ (phi) | N-Cα bond | Freely rotates |
| ψ (psi) | Cα-C bond | Freely rotates |
| ω (omega) | C-N peptide bond | Restricted (~180°) |

These angles determine secondary structures (helices, sheets).

## Peptide Bonds and Drug Stability

### Challenges
- Enzymatic degradation in gut (oral peptides)
- Plasma proteases reduce half-life
- Immune recognition possible

### Solutions in Drug Design
- **Cyclic peptides** - Protect N and C termini
- **D-amino acids** - Enzymes don't recognize
- **N-methylation** - Blocks enzyme access
- **Pseudopeptide bonds** - Modified bond chemistry

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between a peptide bond and a disulfide bond?

Peptide bonds link amino acids in sequence (backbone). Disulfide bonds form between cysteine residues, connecting different parts of the chain (or different chains). Peptide bonds create the chain; disulfide bonds help fold and stabilize it.

### Why can't most peptides be taken orally?

Digestive enzymes (proteases) efficiently break peptide bonds. The stomach and intestines are designed to break down proteins into amino acids for absorption. Most peptides are rapidly degraded before they can be absorbed intact.

### How do peptide drugs survive in the body?

Drug designers modify peptides to resist breakdown: using D-amino acids (enzyme-invisible), adding protective groups, creating cyclic structures, attaching to carriers like albumin, or using non-natural amino acids at susceptible positions.
