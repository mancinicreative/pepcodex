Research Definition

Pharmacodynamics

Also known as: PD, Drug dynamics

Pharmacodynamics is the study of how a drug affects the body, including its mechanism of action, therapeutic effects, and side effects. Pharmacodynamics answers 'what the drug does to the body' and complements pharmacokinetics to provide a complete picture of drug behavior.

Last updated: January 20, 2026

Pharmacodynamics vs Pharmacokinetics

AspectPharmacokinetics (PK)Pharmacodynamics (PD)
QuestionWhat does body do to drug?What does drug do to body?
FocusDrug concentrationDrug effect
MeasuresADME, half-life, AUCEfficacy, potency, response
VariablesTime, concentrationDose, response

Key Pharmacodynamic Concepts

Receptor Binding

  • Drug must bind to target (receptor, enzyme, etc.)
  • Affinity: how strongly it binds
  • Selectivity: which receptors it prefers

Dose-Response Relationship

  • Describes relationship between dose and effect
  • Usually follows sigmoid curve
  • Key parameters: EC50, Emax

Therapeutic Index

  • Range between therapeutic and toxic doses
  • Wider = safer drug
  • Narrow index requires careful monitoring

Important PD Parameters

ParameterDefinitionSignificance
EC50Dose producing 50% max effectMeasure of potency

Peptide Pharmacodynamics Examples

GLP-1 Agonists (Semaglutide)

Targets: GLP-1 receptor Effects:

  • Enhanced insulin secretion (glucose-dependent)
  • Suppressed glucagon
  • Slowed gastric emptying
  • Reduced appetite (CNS effects)

Growth Hormone Secretagogues (Ipamorelin)

Targets: GHS-R (ghrelin receptor) Effects:

  • Stimulates pituitary GH release
  • Increases IGF-1 (downstream)
  • Minimal cortisol/prolactin effects (selectivity)

Dose-Response Curves

   | /

        Dose →

Curve Interpretation

  • Left shift: More potent (works at lower doses)
  • Right shift: Less potent (needs higher doses)
  • Steeper curve: Narrow dosing range
  • Plateau height: Maximum possible effect

PD in Drug Development

Preclinical Studies

  • Cell studies (receptor binding, activation)
  • Animal studies (effects, dose-finding)
  • Establish mechanism of action

Clinical Studies

  • Phase II: Find optimal dose
  • Phase III: Confirm efficacy
  • Monitor expected and unexpected effects

PK/PD Modeling

Modern drug development combines both:

Dose → PK (Concentration) → PD (Effect) → Outcome

Understanding this relationship helps:

  • Optimize dosing regimens
  • Predict drug interactions
  • Individualize therapy

Frequently Asked Questions

Why might two people respond differently to the same dose?

Pharmacodynamic variability includes differences in receptor density, receptor sensitivity, downstream signaling, and individual physiology. Even with identical drug levels (PK), the effect (PD) can vary between individuals.

What does “potent” mean for a drug?

Potency refers to the dose needed to produce an effect. A more potent drug produces the same effect at a lower dose. Potency doesn’t equal effectiveness—a less potent drug at adequate dose can be equally effective.

How do side effects relate to pharmacodynamics?

Side effects occur when a drug affects systems beyond the intended target. This can be due to: receptor presence in multiple tissues (on-target/off-tissue), activity at unintended receptors (off-target), or downstream pathway effects.
