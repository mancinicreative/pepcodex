Semax

Also known as: ACTH(4-7)PGP, Methionyl-Glutamyl-Histidyl-Phenylalanyl-Prolyl-Glycyl-Proline, MEHFPGP

A synthetic heptapeptide analogue of ACTH(4-7) with a C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro extension, developed by Russian researchers and approved in Russia since ~1997 for stroke, cognitive impairment, and optic nerve disorders. Demonstrates nootropic and neuroprotective effects through BDNF/NGF upregulation, dopaminergic and serotonergic modulation, and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. Extensive Russian clinical research supports efficacy, particularly in stroke rehabilitation, though Western independent replication remains limited.

Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Synthetic heptapeptide (7 amino acids) derived from ACTH(4-7) with C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro extension
  • Approved in Russia since ~1997 for ischemic stroke, cognitive disorders, optic nerve atrophy, and dyscirculatory encephalopathy
  • Listed on Russian List of Vital & Essential Drugs (approved December 7, 2011)
  • Not approved by FDA, EMA, or other Western regulatory agencies
  • Multi-target mechanism: Nootropic effects via BDNF/NGF upregulation, dopamine/serotonin modulation, and immunomodulatory/anti-inflammatory activity
  • Unique profile: Neuroprotective without hormonal (cortisol-stimulating) activity of parent ACTH molecule
  • Developed by: Institute of Molecular Genetics, Russian Academy of Sciences (Academicians I.P. Ashmarin and N.F. Myasoedov)

Evidence Strength: Moderate - Extensive Russian clinical trials and over 25 years of post-marketing experience in Russia. Included on the Russian List of Vital & Essential Drugs. However, most research originates from Russian institutions with limited independent Western replication.

Research Timeline

  • 1960s-1970s: Soviet research on ACTH fragments and their effects on learning and memory
  • 1980s: Semax synthesized at Institute of Molecular Genetics, Russian Academy of Sciences under guidance of Academicians I.P. Ashmarin and N.F. Myasoedov
  • 1991: First description of Semax in scientific literature
  • 1997: Approved in Russia for clinical use (stroke, cognitive disorders)
  • 2000s: Extensive clinical trials for stroke rehabilitation, optic nerve disorders
  • 2011: Added to Russian List of Vital & Essential Drugs (December 7, 2011)
  • 2014-present: Genome-wide transcriptomic studies elucidating molecular mechanisms
  • Current: Available as prescription medication in Russia and Ukraine; research chemical elsewhere

Mechanism of Action

Semax has a complex, multi-target mechanism of action that distinguishes it from traditional nootropics. Unlike its parent ACTH molecule, Semax does not stimulate cortisol release, making it safe for long-term neurological use.

How Semax Works

Think of Semax as a “brain fertilizer and protector” that works through several complementary pathways:

  1. Boosts BDNF and NGF - Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and nerve growth factor (NGF) are like fertilizers for your neurons. Semax rapidly increases expression of both, supporting learning, memory, neuronal survival, and recovery from injury. Studies show up to 8-fold increase in BDNF mRNA within 30 minutes.

  2. Activates dopamine and serotonin systems - These neurotransmitters regulate attention, motivation, mood, and cognitive function. Semax enhances serotonin turnover and modulates dopaminergic activity, particularly in the striatum and frontal cortex.

  3. Reduces inflammation - Following brain injury (like stroke), Semax suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1a, IL-1b, IL-6) and chemokines, protecting neurons from inflammatory damage.

  4. Enhances immune response - Paradoxically, while suppressing harmful inflammation, Semax enhances beneficial immune functions including antigen presentation and immunoglobulin synthesis.

  5. Modulates gene expression - Semax affects hundreds of genes involved in neurotransmission, immune response, vascular function, and cell survival, producing lasting changes through transcriptional regulation.

Why this matters: Unlike simple stimulants that deplete neurotransmitters, Semax supports the brain’s own repair and enhancement mechanisms through neurotrophic factors and gene expression changes, producing sustained benefits without tolerance or dependence.

Evidence Highlights

  1. Russian Regulatory Approval: Approved prescription medication in Russia since ~1997; added to List of Vital & Essential Drugs in 2011
  2. Stroke Rehabilitation: Clinical trials showing improved functional recovery, increased BDNF levels, better Barthel index scores
  3. Optic Nerve Disorders: Clinical studies demonstrating improved visual acuity and visual field in optic neuropathy patients
  4. Mechanistic Research: Comprehensive genome-wide transcriptomic studies characterizing molecular mechanisms
  5. Safety Profile: Favorable safety in clinical trials and 25+ years of post-marketing experience; low toxicity

Key Strengths of Evidence

  • Regulatory approval in a major country (Russia) with ongoing surveillance
  • List of Vital & Essential Drugs designation (2011)
  • Multiple clinical indications with supporting trials
  • Comprehensive mechanistic data including genome-wide studies
  • Long post-marketing experience (25+ years)
  • Consistent neurotrophic effects across multiple models

Key Limitations of Evidence

  • Geographic concentration: Most research from Russian institutions
  • Limited Western replication: Few independent studies outside Russia/CIS
  • Language barrier: Many primary studies published only in Russian
  • No FDA/EMA trials: Western regulatory pathway not pursued
  • Publication bias: Negative Russian studies may be underreported
  • GLP/GCP standards: Historical studies may not meet current Western regulatory standards

Russian Approved Indications

Since approval in Russia (~1997), Semax has accumulated significant clinical experience:

  • Primary indications: Ischemic stroke, dyscirculatory encephalopathy, cognitive disorders
  • Secondary indications: Optic nerve atrophy, transient cerebral circulatory disorders
  • Formulations: 0.1% solution (standard), 1% solution (high-dose)
  • Dosing: 200-2000 mcg per dose (3-30 mcg/kg), 2-3 times daily
  • Typical course: 10-14 days, may repeat after interval
  • Adverse event profile: Favorable; no serious safety signals reported
  • No withdrawal syndrome: Safe discontinuation

In Vitro Studies

  • Glial cell cultures: Rapid induction of BDNF and NGF mRNAs in rat glial cell cultures
  • Enkephalinase inhibition: Inhibits enkephalin-degrading enzymes (IC50 ~10 μM)
  • Amyloid aggregation: Prevents copper-induced Abeta aggregation in membrane models
  • Melanocortin receptors: Antagonizes alpha-MSH at MC4/MC5 receptors

Advantages of Semax vs. ACTH

The C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro extension provides:

  1. Metabolic stability: Resistance to proteolytic degradation
  2. No hormonal activity: Does not stimulate cortisol release (unlike ACTH)
  3. CNS penetration: Enhanced ability to cross blood-brain barrier intranasally
  4. Prolonged action: Effects persist beyond plasma half-life through gene expression changes
  5. Independent activity: PGP tripeptide itself has neuroprotective properties

Potential Concerns

  • Limited long-term data: Western-standard long-term studies lacking
  • Intranasal administration: Nasal cavity discoloration possible with chronic use
  • Diabetic patients: Monitor blood glucose (possible elevation)
  • Quality control: Products outside Russia may have variable quality
  • Drug interactions: Not comprehensively studied
  • Immunomodulation: Theoretical concerns with chronic immune modification

Contraindications (Per Russian Labeling)

  • Pregnancy and lactation (insufficient data)
  • Acute psychoses
  • Convulsive disorders (relative; use with caution)
  • Malignant hypertension

Clinical Implications

The rapid metabolism to active fragments means:

  1. Gene expression mechanism: Effects depend on transcriptional changes, not sustained plasma levels
  2. No accumulation: Low risk of build-up with repeated dosing
  3. Nasal delivery optimal: Rapid absorption, bypasses first-pass metabolism, direct CNS access
  4. Multiple active species: Both parent and PGP metabolite contribute to effects
  5. Sustained benefits: Effects persist through downstream gene expression changes

Regulatory Pathway Notes

Unlike Western pharmaceuticals, Semax was developed entirely within the Russian/Soviet research system:

  • Developed at Institute of Molecular Genetics (IMG), Russian Academy of Sciences
  • Key researchers: Academicians I.P. Ashmarin and N.F. Myasoedov
  • Clinical trials conducted per Russian regulatory requirements
  • Approved by Russian Ministry of Health (~1997)
  • No attempt to pursue FDA or EMA approval (different regulatory standards, costs)
  • Research continues at IMG and affiliated Russian institutions
  • Lack of GLP/GCP standardization in historical studies limits Western regulatory acceptance
