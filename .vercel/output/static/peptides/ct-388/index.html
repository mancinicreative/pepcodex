Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Dual agonist of GLP-1 and GIP receptors (same mechanism class as tirzepatide)
  • Developer: Roche (acquired via Carmot Therapeutics for $2.7 billion in 2024)

The Roche-Carmot Acquisition

In December 2023, Roche announced the acquisition of Carmot Therapeutics for approximately $2.7 billion upfront, with potential milestone payments up to $400 million. The deal closed in April 2024.

Why Roche acquired Carmot:

  1. CT-388 - The lead subcutaneous GLP-1/GIP dual agonist (this compound)
  2. CT-996 - An oral GLP-1 receptor agonist with differentiated mechanism
  3. Platform technology - Carmot’s peptide engineering expertise
  4. Strategic entry - Roche’s first major move into the obesity therapeutic space

This acquisition represents one of the largest deals in the obesity drug space and signals Big Pharma’s conviction in the long-term potential of incretin-based therapies beyond Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk.

Mechanism of Action

How CT-388 Works (Plain English)

CT-388 works by activating TWO hormone receptors simultaneously, similar to how Mounjaro (tirzepatide) works:

  1. GLP-1 receptor activation:

    • Tells your brain you’re full after eating less food
    • Slows down stomach emptying so you stay satisfied longer
    • Helps your pancreas release insulin when blood sugar is high
    • This is the same pathway that Ozempic and Wegovy use

  2. GIP receptor activation:

    • Works together with GLP-1 to enhance insulin release
    • May directly improve how your fat cells function
    • Potentially contributes to greater weight loss than GLP-1 alone

Why two receptors matter: Think of it like having two volume controls for the “I’m full” signal instead of one. By activating both GLP-1 and GIP receptors, CT-388 creates a stronger combined effect on appetite and metabolism.

Key insight: The 18.8% weight loss at just 24 weeks is particularly impressive because weight loss with these drugs typically continues beyond 24 weeks. This suggests CT-388 may have rapid early efficacy that could translate to competitive results in longer trials.

GLP-1/GIP Dual Agonist Science

CT-388 belongs to the same mechanistic class as tirzepatide - a dual incretin agonist targeting both GLP-1R and GIPR.

The Incretin System

When you eat, your gut releases two key hormones:

  • GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1) - Accounts for ~35-40% of the incretin effect
  • GIP (Glucose-Dependent Insulinotropic Polypeptide) - Accounts for ~60-65% of the incretin effect

Historical note: GIP was actually discovered first and is responsible for a LARGER portion of natural postprandial insulin secretion, yet GLP-1-only drugs dominated early development.

Critical distinction: White adipose tissue has GIP receptors but minimal GLP-1 receptors. This means dual agonists like CT-388 can directly influence fat cell behavior in ways that GLP-1-only drugs cannot.

Comparison to Tirzepatide

Both CT-388 and tirzepatide are dual GLP-1/GIP agonists, but they may differ in:

  • Receptor binding affinities and balance
  • Pharmacokinetic profiles
  • Specific structural modifications
  • Proprietary peptide engineering

Tirzepatide is “imbalanced” with ~5x greater GIPR potency vs native GIP. CT-388’s specific receptor balance has not been publicly disclosed, which may contribute to any efficacy or tolerability differences.
