GHRP-2

Also known as: Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide-2, Pralmorelin, KP-102

A synthetic hexapeptide GH secretagogue with the strongest GH-releasing potency in its class. More potent than GHRP-6 with different side effect profile: less appetite stimulation but greater cortisol and prolactin elevation. Approved in Japan as Pralmorelin for GH deficiency diagnosis. Extensively studied in human pharmacology with robust clinical data.

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Most potent first-generation growth hormone secretagogue in the GHRP family
  • Approved in Japan as Pralmorelin (KP-102) for diagnosis of GH deficiency since 2004
  • Less selective than ipamorelin - increases cortisol and prolactin more than GHRP-6
  • Weaker appetite stimulation than GHRP-6 despite similar ghrelin receptor mechanism
  • Extensive human pharmacology data from Italian, Japanese, and American research groups
  • Not approved for therapeutic use in any jurisdiction; diagnostic use only (Japan)
  • WADA prohibited - banned in competitive sports

Evidence Strength: MODERATE - Robust human pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic data exists. Approved for diagnostic use in Japan with regulatory-grade safety data. More human studies than most research peptides, but no completed Phase 3 therapeutic efficacy trials outside diagnostic applications.

Why GHRP-2 Was Developed

GHRP-2 emerged from systematic optimization of the original GHRP structure:

Development rationale:

  1. Enhanced potency: GHRP-6 was effective but researchers sought greater GH-releasing activity
  2. Structural optimization: Substitution of D-Alanine at position 1 and D-2-Naphthylalanine at position 2
  3. Improved receptor binding: Modified structure showed ~3x greater potency than GHRP-6
  4. Result: Most potent hexapeptide GH secretagogue developed

Key structural differences from GHRP-6:

PositionGHRP-6GHRP-2Effect
1HisD-AlaEnhanced stability
2D-TrpD-2-NalIncreased receptor affinity
3-6SameSameCore pharmacophore retained

Clinical development path: Unlike GHRP-6 which remained purely research, GHRP-2 achieved regulatory approval in Japan for diagnostic purposes due to its consistent, potent GH-releasing effect.

Mechanism of Action

How GHRP-2 Works

GHRP-2 triggers growth hormone release by activating the ghrelin receptor. Here’s what that means:

  1. Activates the ghrelin receptor - Your body has a receptor called GHS-R1a (the “ghrelin receptor”) that responds to ghrelin, the “hunger hormone.” GHRP-2 mimics ghrelin’s action on this receptor.

  2. Causes powerful GH release - GHRP-2 is the most potent hexapeptide GH secretagogue. When it binds to ghrelin receptors on pituitary cells, it triggers robust growth hormone release.

  3. Not very selective - Unlike ipamorelin, GHRP-2 also significantly increases cortisol and prolactin. In fact, GHRP-2 raises cortisol and prolactin MORE than GHRP-6 does.

  4. Paradoxically less hunger effect - Despite activating the same receptor as ghrelin (the hunger hormone), GHRP-2 causes LESS appetite stimulation than GHRP-6. This pharmacological paradox isn’t fully understood.

Key distinction from GHRP-6: GHRP-2 is more potent for GH release but causes more cortisol/prolactin elevation and less appetite stimulation. The trade-offs make each peptide suited to different research applications.

Diagnostic use: GHRP-2’s potent, consistent GH-releasing effect made it ideal for testing whether someone’s pituitary can produce growth hormone. This is why Japan approved it for diagnosing GH deficiency.

Receptor Targets

Primary: Ghrelin Receptor (GHS-R1a)

Receptor Classification (IUPHAR/BPS Guide to Pharmacology):

  • Official name: Ghrelin receptor (GHSR)
  • Gene: GHSR (human); Ghsr (mouse/rat)
  • Class: Class A G protein-coupled receptor (7 transmembrane domains)
  • Endogenous ligand: Ghrelin (acylated form)
  • GHRP-2 binding: Full agonist with higher potency than GHRP-6
  • Constitutive activity: Yes - receptor maintains basal activity without ligand

Primary Signaling (IUPHAR):

  • Transduction: Gq/G11-mediated phospholipase C stimulation
  • Secondary coupling: Gi/Go and G12/G13
  • Downstream: IP3 accumulation, intracellular Ca2+ release, ERK phosphorylation
  • G protein-independent: Beta-arrestin signaling

Supported Mechanisms (Human Evidence)

GH Release:

  • Most potent hexapeptide GH secretagogue [PMID: 9285939]
  • Acts through PKC and Ca2+ mobilization - cAMP-independent
  • Requires endogenous GHRH for maximal effect - synergistic interaction
  • Dose-dependent response with ceiling effect at higher doses

Hypothalamic Effects:

  • Primary action at hypothalamic level confirmed
  • Stimulates GHRH neuron firing
  • Synergism with exogenous GHRH demonstrated (supra-additive effect)

Non-Selective Effects (distinguishes from ipamorelin):

  • Significantly increases ACTH and cortisol - MORE than GHRP-6 [PMID: 9285939]
  • Significantly increases prolactin - MORE than GHRP-6
  • LESS appetite stimulation than GHRP-6 (pharmacological paradox)

Signaling Pathway Overview

Primary GH-releasing pathway:

GHRP-2 binds GHS-R1a (high affinity)
    |
Gaq/11 protein activation --> PLC activation
    |
IP3 production --> Ca2+ release from ER
    |
+-- Direct: GH vesicle exocytosis (pituitary)
+-- Hypothalamus: GHRH neuron activation --> amplified GH release
+-- CRH pathway: ACTH release --> cortisol secretion

Why more cortisol/prolactin than GHRP-6? The exact mechanism is unclear, but may involve:

  • Different receptor binding kinetics
  • Differential activation of downstream pathways
  • Distinct hypothalamic circuit engagement
  • Possible differences in CRH neuron activation

Contraindications (Theoretical/Extrapolated)

Based on mechanism and regulatory labeling for diagnostic use:

  1. Active malignancy (GH/IGF-1 concerns)
  2. Uncontrolled diabetes (GH can worsen glucose control)
  3. Conditions where cortisol elevation is contraindicated
  4. Hyperprolactinemia or prolactin-sensitive conditions
  5. Known hypersensitivity to GHRP-2 or related peptides

WADA Status

GHRP-2 is PROHIBITED under the World Anti-Doping Agency code.

  • Listed under S2: Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics
  • Specifically: “Growth Hormone Secretagogues (GHS)” including GHRPs
  • Banned both in-competition and out-of-competition
  • Detection methods established via mass spectrometry
  • Metabolite detection extends detection window

Why Limited Development Outside Japan?

  1. Competition from newer compounds: Ipamorelin (more selective), non-peptide GHSs (oral)
  2. Cortisol/prolactin effects: Considered unfavorable for chronic therapy
  3. Limited IP protection: Older peptide with expired patents
  4. Market dynamics: Focus shifted to GHRH analogs (tesamorelin) and oral agents (MK-677)
  5. Diagnostic niche: Japanese approval met specific clinical need
