<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

## Quick Summary

- **FDA-approved glycoprotein hormone** for ovulation induction in ART, cryptorchidism in prepubertal males, and hypogonadotropic hypogonadism
- **Gold standard ovulation trigger** in assisted reproductive technology (IVF/ICSI) with over 50 years of clinical experience
- **Strong evidence base:** Multiple Cochrane systematic reviews, Phase 3 RCTs, and international fertility society guidelines (ASRM, ESHRE)
- **Two formulation types:** Urinary-derived (Pregnyl, Novarel) and recombinant (Ovidrel/choriogonadotropin alfa) with equivalent efficacy
- **Well-characterized safety:** Primary concern is ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS) in susceptible patients; otherwise well-tolerated
- **Physiologic basis:** Mimics luteinizing hormone (LH) action at the LH/hCG receptor to trigger ovulation and support corpus luteum function

**Evidence Strength: HIGH** — Supported by extensive clinical experience spanning decades, multiple Cochrane systematic reviews, FDA approval for specific indications, and international guideline endorsement. One of the most thoroughly characterized hormones in reproductive medicine.

### Molecular Structure

hCG is a **heterodimeric glycoprotein** consisting of two non-covalently linked subunits:

**Alpha subunit (92 amino acids):**
- Common to all glycoprotein hormones (TSH, LH, FSH, hCG)
- Contains two N-linked glycosylation sites
- Provides structural scaffold for receptor binding

**Beta subunit (145 amino acids):**
- Unique to hCG; confers biological specificity
- Contains two N-linked and four O-linked glycosylation sites
- The C-terminal extension (CTP, 24 amino acids) is unique to hCG and prolongs half-life
- 85% sequence homology with LH beta subunit

**Glycosylation significance:**
The extensive glycosylation of hCG serves critical functions:
1. Protects against proteolytic degradation
2. Extends plasma half-life (24-36 hours vs LH's 20 minutes)
3. Influences receptor binding affinity and signaling
4. Creates molecular heterogeneity (multiple isoforms exist)

### Why hCG Was Developed for Fertility

Natural hCG is produced by the placenta during pregnancy to maintain the corpus luteum and support early gestation. Pharmaceutical hCG development arose from several observations:

1. **Physiologic similarity to LH:** hCG binds the same receptor as luteinizing hormone but has a longer half-life (24-36 hours vs ~20 minutes for LH)
2. **Available source:** Pregnant women's urine provided abundant raw material before recombinant technology
3. **Predictable ovulation timing:** Exogenous hCG triggers predictable ovulation 36-40 hours post-administration
4. **Corpus luteum support:** Maintains progesterone production until placenta takes over

**Original therapeutic targets:** Anovulatory infertility and cryptorchidism (undescended testes). The fertility application expanded dramatically with the development of IVF in the late 1970s.

<h2 id="mechanism-of-action">Mechanism of Action</h2>

### How hCG Works (Plain English)

hCG is sometimes called "the pregnancy hormone," but its pharmaceutical use is about mimicking a different hormone called LH (luteinizing hormone). Here's what it does:

1. **Triggers ovulation** — In women undergoing fertility treatment, hCG tells the mature egg follicles "time to release." This happens about 36-40 hours after injection, giving doctors a precise window to retrieve eggs for IVF or time intercourse/insemination.

2. **Supports the corpus luteum** — After ovulation, the empty follicle becomes the corpus luteum, which produces progesterone to prepare the uterus for pregnancy. hCG keeps this structure alive and functioning, like keeping the "pregnancy support system" running.

3. **Stimulates testosterone production (in males)** — In men and boys, hCG tells the testicles to produce testosterone. This is useful for treating undescended testicles in boys or maintaining testicular function in men with certain hormonal conditions.

4. **Mimics LH with a longer duration** — Natural LH disappears from the blood within 20 minutes. hCG lasts 24-36 hours, providing sustained stimulation. It's like comparing a camera flash to leaving the lights on.

**Why hCG works better than LH for treatment:** The body clears natural LH too quickly for practical use. hCG has a special "tail" (the C-terminal peptide) and sugar molecules attached that make it last much longer, so one injection provides the sustained signal needed for ovulation or testosterone production.

### Receptor Target: LH/hCG Receptor (Scientific Detail)

hCG binds to the **lutropin/choriogonadotropin receptor (LHCGR)**, a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) of the rhodopsin-like (Class A) family. This receptor is shared with luteinizing hormone (LH).

**LHCGR expression and functional effects:**

| Tissue | Expression Level | Primary Effect | Clinical Relevance |
|--------|------------------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Ovarian granulosa cells** | High | Triggers final oocyte maturation | Ovulation induction |
| **Ovarian theca cells** | High | Stimulates androgen synthesis | Estrogen precursors |
| **Corpus luteum** | Very high | Maintains progesterone secretion | Luteal support |
| **Leydig cells (testis)** | High | Stimulates testosterone synthesis | Hypogonadism, cryptorchidism |
| **Uterus** | Moderate | Affects implantation, myometrial quiescence | Pregnancy support |
| **Extragonadal tissues** | Low | Various (angiogenesis, immune modulation) | Research interest |

### Signaling Pathway Overview

**Primary pathway (Galphas/cAMP):**
```
hCG binds LHCGR extracellular domain
    |
Receptor activation (transmembrane conformational change)
    |
Galphas protein activation --> adenylyl cyclase --> cAMP increase
    |
Protein kinase A (PKA) activation
    |
+-- Granulosa cells: Triggers meiosis resumption, cumulus expansion
+-- Theca cells: Androstenedione synthesis
+-- Corpus luteum: Progesterone synthesis enzymes (StAR, P450scc)
+-- Leydig cells: Testosterone biosynthesis (StAR, P450c17)
```

**In simpler terms:** When hCG binds to its receptor, it triggers a cascade of signals inside the cell. The key messenger is cAMP (cyclic AMP), which acts like an "on switch" for various enzymes. In the ovary, this leads to ovulation and progesterone production. In the testis, it leads to testosterone production.

**Secondary pathways:** LHCGR also activates:
- Phospholipase C/IP3 pathway (calcium signaling)
- MAPK/ERK pathway (gene transcription, cell differentiation)
- Beta-arrestin recruitment (receptor internalization, alternative signaling)

### Supported Mechanisms (Direct Human Evidence)

**Ovulation triggering:**
- hCG injection induces LH surge-like effects [Cochrane PMID: 25358904]
- Meiosis resumption occurs within 24-36 hours in mature follicles
- Cumulus-oocyte complex expansion enables oocyte release
- Ovulation timing is highly predictable (36-40 hours post-injection)

**Luteal support:**
- hCG maintains corpus luteum steroidogenesis [PMID: 20735820]
- Progesterone levels sustained above 10 ng/mL threshold for implantation
- Effect duration matches hCG half-life; may need repeated doses

**Testicular stimulation:**
- hCG stimulates Leydig cell testosterone production [PMID: 15713727]
- Maintains intratesticular testosterone during exogenous testosterone use
- May support spermatogenesis by maintaining seminiferous tubule environment

### hCG vs LH: Key Differences

| Property | hCG | LH |
|----------|-----|-----|
| **Source** | Placenta (natural); pregnant urine or recombinant (pharmaceutical) | Pituitary gland |
| **Half-life** | 24-36 hours | ~20 minutes |
| **Receptor** | LHCGR | LHCGR (same receptor) |
| **Glycosylation** | Extensive (8 sites) | Less (2 sites) |
| **Potency** | Slightly higher receptor affinity | Lower affinity, faster clearance |
| **Clinical use** | Pharmaceutical (ovulation trigger) | Endogenous only |

**Why the half-life difference matters:** LH's short half-life is physiologically appropriate for pulsatile hypothalamic-pituitary signaling. hCG's long half-life allows a single injection to provide sustained receptor stimulation equivalent to the natural LH surge, which can last 48-50 hours.

<h2 id="physiology-context">Too Much vs Too Little: Physiologic Context</h2>

### Native hCG Physiology

**In pregnancy:**
- hCG is produced by trophoblast cells beginning at implantation (~6-7 days post-fertilization)
- Levels double every 48-72 hours in early pregnancy
- Peak levels: 50,000-100,000 mIU/mL at 8-10 weeks gestation
- Primary function: Maintains corpus luteum progesterone production until placenta takes over (~10-12 weeks)

**Normal non-pregnant state:**
- hCG is undetectable or &lt;5 mIU/mL
- Detection indicates pregnancy (or rarely, certain tumors)

### LH Physiology (What hCG Mimics)

**Normal menstrual cycle:**
- Pulsatile LH release throughout follicular phase
- LH surge (10-20x baseline) triggers ovulation mid-cycle
- Surge lasts ~48 hours; ovulation occurs ~36 hours after surge onset
- Luteal phase LH supports corpus luteum initially

**In men:**
- Pulsatile LH maintains Leydig cell testosterone production
- LH pulses occur approximately every 90-120 minutes
- Feedback loop with testosterone regulates secretion

### What Happens When hCG Is Used Therapeutically

**Ovulation induction setting:**
- Single injection of hCG (5,000-10,000 IU urinary; 250 mcg recombinant)
- Creates "artificial LH surge" lasting 24-36 hours
- More predictable and controllable than waiting for natural surge
- Risk: Excessive stimulation can cause OHSS in susceptible patients

**Corpus luteum support:**
- Multiple hCG injections or progesterone supplementation
- Maintains luteal function when natural LH is suppressed
- Common after GnRH agonist protocols in ART

**Male hypogonadism setting:**
- hCG stimulates testicular testosterone production
- Used when pituitary function is impaired (hypogonadotropic hypogonadism)
- Can maintain spermatogenesis when combined with FSH

### Tolerance and Adaptation

| Observation | Evidence | Clinical Implication |
|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| **Receptor desensitization** | Chronic high-dose hCG can downregulate LHCGR | Pulsatile or intermittent dosing preferred in long-term use |
| **Antibody formation** | Rare with modern preparations | More common historically with impure urinary products |
| **Ovarian hyperstimulation** | Risk highest with high hCG + multiple follicles | Requires careful monitoring and patient selection |
| **Testicular desensitization** | Can occur with excessive dosing in males | Appropriate dose titration important |

**In simpler terms:** The body's cells can become less responsive to hCG if given too much for too long. This is why fertility doctors use single trigger doses rather than continuous infusions, and why long-term male use requires careful dosing. The receptor essentially "turns down the volume" when overstimulated.

<h2 id="key-human-trials">Key Human Trials</h2>

### Ovulation Trigger Comparisons (Cochrane Review)

**Youssef et al. 2014 [PMID: 25358904]** — Definitive Cochrane systematic review comparing hCG trigger vs GnRH agonist trigger in assisted reproduction.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Design** | Systematic review of 17 RCTs |
| **N** | 1,847 women |
| **Population** | Women undergoing IVF/ICSI with GnRH antagonist protocols |
| **Primary outcome** | Live birth rate; OHSS incidence |
| **hCG live birth rate** | Higher than GnRH agonist trigger |
| **OHSS risk** | Significantly higher with hCG (OR 3.54) |

**Key conclusions:**
- hCG trigger produces higher live birth rates than GnRH agonist trigger in standard settings
- GnRH agonist trigger virtually eliminates OHSS but at cost of lower pregnancy rates (unless modified luteal support used)
- hCG remains gold standard trigger when OHSS risk is acceptable
- In high OHSS risk patients, consider GnRH agonist trigger with intensive luteal support

### Recombinant vs Urinary hCG

**Al-Inany et al. Cochrane 2005 [PMID: 15846678]**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Design** | Systematic review of RCTs |
| **N** | 2,000+ women |
| **Comparison** | Recombinant hCG (rhCG) vs urinary hCG (uhCG) |
| **Primary outcome** | Ongoing pregnancy/live birth per woman randomized |

**Key findings:**
- No significant difference in pregnancy rates between rhCG and uhCG
- No significant difference in OHSS rates
- rhCG offers more consistent product quality (batch-to-batch uniformity)
- Patient preference may favor rhCG (prefilled syringe, subcutaneous administration)

**European Recombinant hCG Study Group 2000 [PMID: 10875848]**

Pivotal Phase 3 trial establishing rhCG equivalence to uhCG:
- 228 women undergoing ART randomized to rhCG (250 mcg) vs uhCG (5,000 IU)
- Similar number of oocytes retrieved
- Similar fertilization and pregnancy rates
- Led to FDA approval of Ovidrel (choriogonadotropin alfa)

### hCG in Cryptorchidism

**Pyorala et al. Meta-analysis 1995 [PMID: 7673426]**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Design** | Meta-analysis of 33 studies |
| **Population** | Boys with cryptorchidism (undescended testes) |
| **Treatment** | Various hCG regimens |
| **Success rate** | ~19% overall descent rate |

**Key findings:**
- hCG treatment achieves testicular descent in approximately 1 in 5 cases
- Success rate higher in retractile testes than true cryptorchidism
- Most guidelines now recommend surgery (orchiopexy) as primary treatment
- hCG may still be used in selected cases or pre-operatively

### hCG for Intratesticular Testosterone Preservation

**Coviello et al. 2005 [PMID: 15713727]**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Design** | Randomized controlled trial |
| **N** | 29 normal men |
| **Intervention** | Testosterone + varying hCG doses |
| **Primary outcome** | Intratesticular testosterone (ITT) |

**Key findings:**
- Exogenous testosterone suppresses endogenous LH, collapsing intratesticular testosterone
- hCG 125 IU every other day maintained ITT at 25% of baseline
- hCG 250 IU every other day maintained ITT at ~50% of baseline
- hCG 500 IU every other day restored ITT to near-normal levels
- Supports hCG use for fertility preservation in men on testosterone therapy

### OHSS Prevention Guidelines

**ASRM Practice Committee 2016 [PMID: 27678032]**

Key recommendations regarding hCG:
1. Identify high-risk patients (PCOS, high AFC, prior OHSS)
2. Consider GnRH agonist trigger instead of hCG in highest-risk patients
3. If hCG used in at-risk patients, consider lower dose (5,000 IU instead of 10,000 IU)
4. "Freeze-all" strategy (no fresh transfer) reduces OHSS severity
5. Dopamine agonists (cabergoline) may reduce OHSS risk when hCG trigger used

### In Vitro Studies

**Receptor binding:**
- hCG binds LHCGR with high affinity (Kd ~0.1-1 nM)
- Slightly higher affinity than native LH
- Both subunits required for receptor activation
- Glycosylation affects binding kinetics and signaling potency

**Cell signaling:**
- Primary cAMP pathway activation confirmed in multiple cell types
- Dose-dependent steroidogenesis in granulosa/theca/Leydig cell cultures
- Receptor internalization and recycling characterized

### Limitations of Preclinical Data

- Rodent ovarian physiology differs from humans (multiple ovulation normal)
- Primate studies most relevant but limited by ethical/practical constraints
- In vitro systems may not fully replicate in vivo complexity
- Species differences in LHCGR expression patterns

### Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome: Detailed Assessment

**What is OHSS:**
OHSS is an iatrogenic complication of ovarian stimulation characterized by:
- Ovarian enlargement with multiple cysts
- Fluid shift from intravascular to third space (ascites, pleural effusion)
- Hemoconcentration, electrolyte disturbances
- In severe cases: renal failure, thromboembolic events, ARDS

**OHSS Classification:**

| Grade | Symptoms | Management |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Mild** | Abdominal bloating, mild pain, ovaries &lt;8 cm | Outpatient monitoring |
| **Moderate** | Nausea, vomiting, ultrasound evidence of ascites | Close monitoring, hydration |
| **Severe** | Tense ascites, oliguria, hemoconcentration | Hospitalization required |
| **Critical** | Renal failure, ARDS, thromboembolism | ICU care |

**Risk factors for OHSS:**
1. Young age (less than 35 years)
2. Low body weight
3. Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
4. High antral follicle count (>24)
5. High estradiol levels during stimulation
6. Large number of follicles (>25)
7. Previous OHSS history
8. Use of hCG for trigger (vs GnRH agonist)

**Why hCG contributes to OHSS:**
- hCG's long half-life provides sustained LHCGR stimulation
- Continued stimulation of multiple follicles and corpora lutea
- VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor) release drives capillary leak
- Endogenous hCG from early pregnancy perpetuates and worsens OHSS

**Prevention strategies:**
- GnRH agonist trigger in high-risk patients
- Coasting (withholding gonadotropins) if estradiol very high
- Freeze-all embryo strategy (avoids pregnancy hCG contribution)
- Lower hCG trigger doses
- Dopamine agonist co-treatment

### Contraindications

1. **Primary ovarian failure** (elevated FSH, no follicle development)
2. **Uncontrolled thyroid or adrenal dysfunction**
3. **Sex hormone-dependent tumors** (certain breast, ovarian, prostate cancers)
4. **Abnormal uterine bleeding of undetermined origin**
5. **Ovarian cyst or enlargement** not due to PCOS
6. **Prior allergic reaction** to hCG products

### Special Populations

| Population | Recommendation | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-------|
| **Pregnancy** | Contraindicated for ovulation induction | Natural hCG rises; exogenous not needed |
| **Breastfeeding** | Limited data | Generally avoided during active breastfeeding |
| **Renal impairment** | Caution | May affect clearance; monitor closely |
| **Hepatic impairment** | Caution | Limited data |
| **Prepubertal males** | FDA approved (cryptorchidism) | Specific dosing for pediatric use |

### Clinical Dosing Context

**Ovulation trigger (standard IVF):**
- Urinary hCG: 5,000-10,000 IU intramuscularly
- Recombinant hCG: 250 mcg subcutaneously
- Single injection administered 34-36 hours before scheduled oocyte retrieval

**Luteal support:**
- If used (vs progesterone): 1,500-2,500 IU every 3-4 days
- Risk: May exacerbate OHSS; progesterone often preferred

**Cryptorchidism (prepubertal males):**
- Various regimens; typically 1,000-4,000 IU 2-3 times weekly for 4-6 weeks
- Consult current pediatric endocrinology guidelines

**Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (adult males):**
- 1,000-2,000 IU 2-3 times weekly
- Often combined with FSH (hMG or recombinant FSH) for spermatogenesis

<h2 id="inferences-section">Inferences Section</h2>

*This section contains logical reasoning from known evidence. These are NOT proven facts.*

### Inference 1: Lower hCG doses may maintain efficacy while reducing OHSS risk

**(a) Statement:** Reducing hCG trigger dose from 10,000 IU to 5,000 IU (or 250 mcg to 125 mcg rhCG) may reduce OHSS incidence without significantly compromising pregnancy rates.

**(b) Why someone might believe it:**
- OHSS severity correlates with degree and duration of LHCGR stimulation
- Ceiling effect exists for ovulation triggering (more hCG doesn't improve oocyte maturation)
- Some studies suggest lower doses achieve similar pregnancy rates

**(c) Supporting evidence:**
- Dose-response studies show ovulation occurs reliably at lower thresholds
- ASRM guidelines mention considering lower doses in at-risk patients [PMID: 27678032]
- Some clinics report similar outcomes with reduced doses

**(d) Contradicting evidence:**
- Large RCTs specifically comparing doses are limited
- Heterogeneity in patient populations makes comparison difficult
- Some concern about incomplete luteinization at lower doses

**(e) Confidence: MODERATE**

**(f) Study needed:** Adequately powered RCT comparing standard vs reduced hCG doses with live birth rate primary endpoint and OHSS as secondary endpoint.

---

### Inference 2: Dual trigger (GnRH agonist + low-dose hCG) may optimize outcomes

**(a) Statement:** Combining a GnRH agonist trigger with low-dose hCG may provide OHSS protection while improving pregnancy rates compared to GnRH agonist alone.

**(b) Why someone might believe it:**
- GnRH agonist alone reduces OHSS but compromises luteal function
- Adding small hCG dose may provide enough luteal support without full OHSS risk
- Several clinical protocols use this approach

**(c) Supporting evidence:**
- Meta-analyses suggest dual trigger improves outcomes vs GnRH agonist alone in high responders
- Provides both endogenous LH surge (GnRH agonist) and exogenous support (hCG)
- Growing clinical adoption

**(d) Contradicting evidence:**
- Optimal hCG dose for dual trigger not established
- May not fully eliminate OHSS risk in highest-risk patients
- Heterogeneous protocols in literature

**(e) Confidence: MODERATE**

**(f) Study needed:** Standardized RCTs comparing dual trigger protocols with defined hCG doses.

---

### Inference 3: hCG may have direct effects on endometrial receptivity

**(a) Statement:** hCG may directly improve endometrial receptivity independent of corpus luteum progesterone support.

**(b) Why someone might believe it:**
- LHCGR expression detected in endometrium
- hCG may regulate endometrial gene expression
- Some in vitro evidence of direct endometrial effects

**(c) Supporting evidence:**
- LHCGR mRNA and protein detected in human endometrium [multiple studies]
- hCG affects expression of implantation-related genes in endometrial cells
- Intrauterine hCG instillation studies show mixed positive results

**(d) Contradicting evidence:**
- Endometrial LHCGR expression is relatively low
- Clinical trials of intrauterine hCG are inconsistent
- Difficult to separate direct effects from systemic hormonal effects

**(e) Confidence: LOW**

**(f) Study needed:** Well-designed RCT of local vs systemic hCG with endometrial biopsy endpoints.

<h2 id="comparisons">Comparisons</h2>

### hCG vs GnRH Agonist for Ovulation Trigger

| Factor | hCG | GnRH Agonist |
|--------|-----|--------------|
| **Mechanism** | Direct LHCGR stimulation | Induces endogenous LH/FSH surge |
| **Duration of action** | 24-36 hours | 24-36 hours (endogenous surge) |
| **OHSS risk** | Higher | Much lower |
| **Live birth rate (fresh transfer)** | Higher | Lower (without luteal support) |
| **Protocol requirement** | Any antagonist protocol | Requires GnRH antagonist protocol |
| **Luteal support** | May use hCG or progesterone | Requires intensive progesterone |
| **Best use** | Standard risk patients | High OHSS risk patients |

**Cochrane conclusion [PMID: 25358904]:** In fresh autologous cycles, hCG trigger achieves higher live birth rates, but at the cost of significantly higher OHSS risk. GnRH agonist trigger should be considered for high-risk patients, especially if freeze-all strategy is employed.

### Recombinant vs Urinary hCG

| Factor | Recombinant hCG (Ovidrel) | Urinary hCG (Pregnyl, Novarel) |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Source** | CHO cell recombinant | Pregnant women's urine |
| **Purity** | Very high, consistent | Variable, contains other urinary proteins |
| **Administration** | Subcutaneous | Intramuscular |
| **Patient convenience** | Prefilled syringe | Reconstitution required |
| **Efficacy** | Equivalent | Equivalent |
| **OHSS risk** | Similar | Similar |
| **Cost** | Generally higher | Generally lower |
| **Supply reliability** | More reliable | Dependent on collection |

**Clinical bottom line:** No meaningful clinical difference in outcomes. Choice often based on patient preference, cost, and availability.

### hCG vs Progesterone for Luteal Support

| Factor | hCG | Progesterone |
|--------|-----|--------------|
| **Mechanism** | Stimulates corpus luteum to produce progesterone | Direct supplementation |
| **OHSS risk** | May worsen OHSS | Does not affect OHSS |
| **Efficacy** | Equivalent to progesterone | Equivalent to hCG |
| **Administration** | IM injection every 3-4 days | Daily (vaginal, IM, or oral) |
| **Current preference** | Less common | More common |

**Clinical bottom line:** Progesterone supplementation has largely replaced hCG for luteal support due to equivalent efficacy without OHSS exacerbation risk.

### FDA-Approved Indications (Specific)

**Ovulation induction:**
- In anovulatory women who have received appropriate prior treatment with human gonadotropins
- For final follicular maturation and early luteinization in women undergoing ART

**Cryptorchidism (males):**
- Not due to anatomical obstruction
- In prepubertal males

**Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (males):**
- Secondary hypogonadism due to pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction
- May be combined with FSH/hMG for spermatogenesis

### Off-Label Uses (NOT FDA-Approved)

The following uses are **not** FDA-approved and are mentioned for educational purposes only:

- Weight loss (FDA has warned this is ineffective and potentially harmful)
- General testosterone support (when not meeting criteria for hypogonadotropic hypogonadism)
- Anti-aging purposes

### WADA Status

hCG is **prohibited** by the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) in male athletes at all times. It is classified under S2: Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances, and Mimetics.

**Rationale:** hCG can stimulate endogenous testosterone production, providing performance-enhancing effects. Detection methods include measuring hCG in urine.
