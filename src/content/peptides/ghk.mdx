<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

## Quick Summary

- **Naturally occurring tripeptide** (Glycine-Histidine-Lysine)
- **Copper-binding peptide** - in the body, circulates primarily as GHK-Cu complex
- **Endogenous to human plasma** - levels decline significantly with age
- **Base form of GHK-Cu** - most research uses the copper-complexed form
- **Limited research on GHK alone** - copper appears necessary for most biological activity
- **Primarily topical use** - cosmetic applications

**Evidence Strength: LOW** — GHK alone has limited research; most evidence pertains to the copper-complexed form (GHK-Cu). For comprehensive wound healing and cosmetic evidence, see the GHK-Cu dossier.

### Important Distinction: GHK vs GHK-Cu

| Form | Composition | Biological Activity | Commercial Products |
|------|-------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **GHK** | Tripeptide only | Limited without copper | Less common |
| **GHK-Cu** | Tripeptide + copper ion | Full activity | More common in skincare |

**Why this matters:** Most research demonstrating wound healing and anti-aging effects uses GHK-Cu, not GHK alone. The copper ion is essential for several key mechanisms. Products containing only GHK may have reduced efficacy.

<h2 id="mechanism-of-action">Mechanism of Action</h2>

### How GHK Works (Plain English)

GHK is a small protein fragment that your body naturally produces. Think of it as a "signal molecule" that tells cells when tissue is damaged and needs repair. Here's the key point:

**GHK needs copper to work properly.**

When tissue is injured, GHK is released from damaged collagen. In the body, it immediately binds to copper (becoming GHK-Cu), and this complex then:

1. **Signals cells to repair** - Attracts repair cells to injured tissue
2. **Stimulates collagen production** - Helps rebuild the structural matrix
3. **Modulates gene expression** - Affects thousands of genes related to healing

**Without copper:**
- Many key enzyme activities don't occur
- Collagen crosslinking requires copper-dependent enzymes
- Antioxidant effects are reduced

### The Role of Copper

Copper is not just a passenger - it's essential for biological activity:

| Function | Requires Copper? | Mechanism |
|----------|------------------|-----------|
| **Collagen crosslinking** | Yes | Lysyl oxidase is copper-dependent |
| **Antioxidant activity** | Yes | SOD-1 is copper-dependent |
| **Cell signaling** | Probably | Copper delivery to cells |
| **Gene expression effects** | Unclear | May be copper-independent |

### Mechanisms Potentially Independent of Copper

Some GHK effects may not require the copper complex:

| Mechanism | Evidence | Confidence |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Cell chemoattraction** | Some data for GHK alone | Low |
| **Some gene expression** | Theoretical | Very low |
| **Receptor binding** | Hypothesized | Very low |

### Mechanism Summary

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

GHK represents:

**What We Know:**
- Naturally occurring tripeptide discovered in human plasma
- Has high affinity for copper ions
- In the body, circulates primarily as GHK-Cu complex
- Levels decline with age
- Released from damaged collagen during wound healing

**Critical Understanding:**
- **Most biological activity requires the copper complex (GHK-Cu)**
- GHK alone has minimal demonstrated efficacy
- Key mechanisms depend on copper-dependent enzymes
- The natural, functional form is GHK-Cu

**Practical Implications:**
- For cosmetic or wound healing applications, GHK-Cu is preferred
- Products containing only GHK may have reduced efficacy
- The evidence base pertains primarily to GHK-Cu

**Bottom Line:** GHK is scientifically interesting as the base peptide that forms the GHK-Cu complex, but for practical applications, the copper-complexed form (GHK-Cu) is what most research supports. The "low" evidence rating for GHK alone reflects the fact that most research uses GHK-Cu, not a judgment about the well-established GHK-Cu evidence base.
