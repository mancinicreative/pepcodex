BPC-157

Also known as: Body Protection Compound-157, Bepecin, PL 14736, PL-10

A synthetic peptide derived from human gastric juice protein. Preclinical studies suggest tissue repair properties, but human clinical trial data is extremely limited. A 2025 systematic review of 36 studies found only 1 clinical study.

Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Synthetic pentadecapeptide (15 amino acids) derived from human gastric juice protein
  • Not FDA approved for any indication; not approved by any major regulatory agency
  • Preclinical evidence only - nearly all studies are in animal models or cell cultures
  • High interest in fitness/recovery communities despite limited human data
  • Safety profile in humans is not established through rigorous clinical trials

Evidence Strength: Low - Extensive preclinical research but minimal human clinical trial data. Most evidence comes from a single research group.

Research Timeline

  • 1990s: Initial isolation and characterization from gastric juice (Croatian researchers)
  • 1993-present: Preclinical studies primarily from Croatian research group (Sikiric et al.)
  • 2000s: Pliva pharmaceutical company develops PL 14736 for IBD trials
  • 2006: Phase I/II IBD trials referenced but results never fully published
  • 2010s: Independent Taiwanese and Chinese research groups publish supportive findings
  • 2016: Phase I trial (NCT02637284) terminated; no results disclosed
  • 2019: Korean systematic review; UK independent review published
  • 2023: FDA classifies as Category 2 bulk drug substance
  • 2024-2025: First small human pilot studies published; multiple systematic reviews
  • Current: No completed Phase 3 trials; only 2-3 small pilot human studies

Mechanism of Action

The proposed mechanisms of BPC-157 are based primarily on animal and in vitro studies. Human mechanistic data is lacking.

How BPC-157 Works

Think of BPC-157 as a potential “repair signal” that may tell your body to:

  1. Build new blood vessels - Like creating new roads to deliver supplies to a construction site, new blood vessels bring oxygen and nutrients to injured areas so they can heal faster.

  2. Reduce harmful inflammation - Your body’s inflammatory response is like a fire alarm. It’s helpful at first, but if it keeps going, it causes more damage. BPC-157 may help turn down this alarm once it’s no longer needed.

  3. Help repair cells move to injuries - When you have a wound, cells need to migrate (travel) to the damaged area to fix it. BPC-157 may help cells “unstick” from where they are and move to where they’re needed.

  4. Protect the gut lining - The stomach and intestines have a protective barrier. NSAIDs (like ibuprofen) can damage this barrier. In animal studies, BPC-157 helped maintain this protective layer.

Important caveat: These effects have mainly been seen in rats and cell cultures, not in rigorous human studies.

Scientific Pathways (Preclinical Evidence Only)

1. VEGF/VEGFR2 Pathway (Angiogenesis)

BPC-157 → VEGFR2 upregulation & endocytosis
    ↓
PI3K activation
    ↓
AKT phosphorylation
    ↓
eNOS activation → Nitric Oxide
    ↓
├── New blood vessel formation
├── Vasodilation
└── Vascular stability

In simpler terms, BPC-157 appears to increase receptors for a growth factor called VEGF. This triggers a chain reaction inside cells that produces nitric oxide - a molecule that relaxes blood vessels and promotes the growth of new ones. More blood vessels = better nutrient delivery to healing tissues.

Key supporting research: Hsieh MJ et al. (Taiwan, 2017) demonstrated VEGFR2 activation in ischemic wound models. [PMID:28013436]

2. Nitric Oxide System (Dual Pathways)

  • VEGF-dependent: VEGFR2-PI3K-Akt-eNOS cascade
  • VEGF-independent: Src-caveolin-1-eNOS pathway
  • Disrupts caveolin-1 inhibition of eNOS → more NO production
  • Nos1/Nos3 genes upregulated (vasodilation, blood flow)
  • Nos2 downregulated (reduces excessive inflammation)

Nitric oxide (NO) acts like a traffic controller for blood vessels. It tells them to widen so more blood can flow through. BPC-157 may boost the “good” NO that improves blood flow while reducing the “bad” NO that causes excessive inflammation.

Key supporting research: Sikiric P et al. (Croatia, 2014) detailed NO system interactions. [PMID:23772940]

3. FAK-Paxillin Pathway (Cell Migration)

BPC-157 → FAK/Paxillin phosphorylation
    ↓
├── Cell attachment to surfaces
├── Cell migration to injury sites
└── Cell survival under stress

FAK and paxillin are proteins that act like “feet” for cells, helping them grip surfaces and move. When you injure a tendon, the repair cells (fibroblasts) need to travel to the injury site. BPC-157 may activate these cellular “feet,” making it easier for repair cells to reach and fix the damage.

Key supporting research: Chang CH et al. (Taiwan, 2011) demonstrated FAK-paxillin activation in tendon fibroblasts. [PMID:21030672]

4. Growth Hormone Receptor (GHR) Pathway

BPC-157 → GHR upregulation
    ↓
JAK2 phosphorylation
    ↓
STAT5 activation
    ↓
├── Enhanced growth factor signaling
├── Increased collagen synthesis
└── Tissue regeneration

Key supporting research: Chang CH et al. (Taiwan, 2014) demonstrated GHR upregulation in tendon fibroblasts. [PMID:25415472]

5. ERK/MAPK Signaling

  • Upregulates ERK1/2 phosphorylation
  • Activates downstream transcription factors: c-Fos, c-Jun, Egr-1
  • Promotes cell growth, migration, and angiogenesis

ERK/MAPK is essentially an “on switch” for cell growth and repair. When activated, cells get the signal to multiply, move, and build new structures. BPC-157 may flip this switch on.

Key supporting research: Tkalcevic VI et al. (Croatia, 2007) showed Egr-1 involvement in wound healing. [PMID:17628535]

Cytoprotection (Cell Protection)

  • Rescued NSAID-cytotoxicity by stabilizing intestinal permeability
  • First demonstration of NO generation in gastric mucosa → antiulcer effect
  • Hepatoprotective effects in toxin-challenged animal models
  • Endothelial protection from high-glucose damage (diabetic model)

In animal studies, BPC-157 helped protect the gut lining from damage caused by common painkillers like ibuprofen and aspirin. It also appeared to protect liver cells from toxins. The gut has a delicate barrier that keeps stomach acid and bacteria from leaking into your body. BPC-157 may help maintain this barrier.

Key supporting research:

  • Chinese studies (Xue XC et al., 2004; Wang XY et al., 2019) demonstrated GI and hepatic cytoprotection
  • Sun L et al. (China, 2016) showed endothelial protection from hyperglycemia [PMID:27551776]

Important Limitations

  • Nearly all mechanistic studies from single research group (Croatian)
  • Translation to human physiology is unconfirmed
  • Optimal administration route for systemic effects unknown
  • Bioavailability and pharmacokinetics in humans not characterized
  • FDA prohibits sale as drug, supplement, or food ingredient

Key Evidence Highlights

  1. Gastric Protection: Rodent and Chinese studies show ulcer healing acceleration [PMID:15052688]
  2. Tendon/Ligament: Taiwanese studies show improved healing markers [PMID:21030672, PMID:25415472]
  3. Muscle Injury: Croatian animal models suggest faster recovery [PMID:16609966, PMID:18668315]
  4. Corneal Healing: Taiwan/China study showed accelerated wound healing [PMID:25995620]
  5. Neuroprotection: Croatian reviews detail dopaminergic system effects [PMID:27306034]

Critical Note: The overwhelming majority of BPC-157 research comes from one research group in Croatia, and independent replication is limited. Taiwanese, Chinese, and Korean groups have published supportive findings, but these also remain preclinical.

Limitations of Preclinical Data

  • Single research group: Most studies from Croatian laboratory
  • Limited replication: Few independent confirmations (Taiwan, China provide some)
  • Animal models: May not translate to humans
  • Route of administration: Varies widely across studies
  • Publication bias: Negative studies may not be published
  • Lack of head-to-head comparisons: No studies comparing to standard treatments

Potential Concerns

  • Unknown long-term effects: No chronic human exposure data
  • Growth factor effects: Theoretical cancer concerns with pro-angiogenic agents
  • Quality control: Unregulated products may vary in purity
  • Drug interactions: Not studied
  • Contraindications: Not established
  • Receptor activity: D2 receptor agonism may have CNS implications

Important Warning

Products sold as BPC-157 are not regulated pharmaceutical products. Purity, identity, and safety cannot be assured for non-pharmaceutical sources.
