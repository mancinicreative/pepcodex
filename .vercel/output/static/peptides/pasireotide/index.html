Overview

Quick Summary

  • Multi-receptor somatostatin analog targeting SSTR1, SSTR2, SSTR3, and SSTR5 with broader receptor coverage than first-generation analogs
  • FDA approved as Signifor (subcutaneous, 2012) for Cushing’s disease and Signifor LAR (intramuscular, 2014) for acromegaly
  • Superior receptor affinity: 30-fold higher affinity for SSTR1 and 39-fold higher for SSTR5 compared to octreotide
  • PAOLA study demonstrated superiority to octreotide LAR in biochemically uncontrolled acromegaly (31.3% vs 19.2% GH/IGF-1 control)
  • Unique efficacy in resistant disease: Effective in patients inadequately controlled on first-generation somatostatin analogs
  • Notable metabolic effects: Hyperglycemia is the most clinically significant adverse effect, requiring proactive glucose monitoring

Evidence Strength: HIGH — Supported by multiple Phase 3 RCTs (PASPORT, PAOLA, CSOM230B2412), regulatory approvals in multiple jurisdictions, and well-characterized safety profile with over a decade of clinical use.

Why Pasireotide Was Developed

First-generation somatostatin analogs (octreotide, lanreotide) bind primarily to SSTR2, with limited affinity for other receptor subtypes. However:

  1. Receptor heterogeneity: Pituitary adenomas express variable SSTR profiles; many have high SSTR5 expression
  2. Treatment resistance: 25-50% of acromegaly patients are inadequately controlled on octreotide/lanreotide
  3. Cushing’s disease unmet need: No FDA-approved medical therapy existed for Cushing’s disease prior to 2012
  4. Corticotroph adenomas: Express predominantly SSTR5 rather than SSTR2 [PMID: 15315000]

Design rationale: Pasireotide was engineered as a cyclic hexapeptide with high affinity across multiple SSTR subtypes to address these limitations.

Mechanism of Action

How Pasireotide Works (Plain English)

Pasireotide mimics somatostatin, a natural hormone that acts as a “brake” on many glandular secretions. Here’s how it works:

  1. Broader receptor targeting — Your body has five types of somatostatin receptors (SSTR1-5). Older drugs like octreotide mainly work through one type (SSTR2). Pasireotide works through four types (SSTR1, 2, 3, and 5), like having four keys instead of one.

  2. Controls excess cortisol in Cushing’s disease — In Cushing’s disease, a pituitary tumor makes too much ACTH, which causes your adrenal glands to produce excess cortisol. Pasireotide directly suppresses these tumor cells, reducing ACTH and therefore cortisol. The tumor cells have SSTR5 receptors that pasireotide targets effectively.

  3. Controls growth hormone in acromegaly — In acromegaly, a pituitary tumor produces too much growth hormone (GH). Pasireotide suppresses both GH secretion and tumor cell proliferation. Because it works through multiple receptors, it can help patients who don’t respond to octreotide.

  4. Reduces IGF-1 levels — Growth hormone stimulates IGF-1 production in the liver. By reducing GH, pasireotide lowers IGF-1, which drives many acromegaly symptoms.

  5. Important metabolic effect — Pasireotide also reduces insulin secretion (through SSTR5 on pancreatic beta cells), which can raise blood sugar. This is why glucose monitoring is important during treatment.

Why multiple receptors matter: Tumors vary in which receptors they express. By targeting four receptor types, pasireotide works in more patients, including those who failed on single-receptor drugs.

Pasireotide is a pan-somatostatin receptor agonist with highest affinity for SSTR5, followed by SSTR2, SSTR3, and SSTR1.

In simpler terms: When pasireotide binds to somatostatin receptors, it activates “stop” signals inside cells. These signals reduce both hormone secretion (like GH and ACTH) and cell growth. The same mechanism in pancreatic cells reduces insulin release, explaining why blood sugar rises during treatment.

Supported Mechanisms (Direct Human Evidence)

Cushing’s disease:

  • Direct suppression of ACTH secretion from corticotroph adenomas [PMID: 22990096]
  • Consequent reduction in cortisol production
  • Normalization of urinary free cortisol (UFC) in responders

Acromegaly:

  • Suppression of GH secretion from somatotroph adenomas [PMID: 28253614]
  • Secondary reduction in hepatic IGF-1 production
  • Partial tumor shrinkage in some patients

Hyperglycemia mechanism:

  • Direct inhibition of insulin secretion via SSTR5 on beta cells [PMID: 23345097]
  • Reduced incretin effect (GLP-1, GIP suppression)
  • Modest suppression of glucagon (net hyperglycemic effect)

Hyperglycemia: The Critical Safety Consideration

Incidence and severity:

  • New-onset hyperglycemia: 40-50% of patients
  • Diabetes mellitus diagnosis: 20-30% of previously normoglycemic patients
  • HbA1c increase: Mean +1-2% in clinical trials
  • FPG increase: Mean +30-50 mg/dL

Mechanism: Direct SSTR5-mediated inhibition of insulin secretion, with additional suppression of incretin hormones.

Risk factors for severe hyperglycemia:

  • Pre-existing glucose intolerance
  • Higher baseline HbA1c
  • Higher pasireotide dose
  • Concurrent corticosteroid therapy (Cushing’s disease patients)

Management approach:

  1. Pre-treatment: FPG, HbA1c assessment; diabetes risk evaluation
  2. Week 1-4: Weekly FPG monitoring
  3. Ongoing: Monthly FPG; HbA1c every 3 months
  4. Treatment: Metformin first-line; DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists, or insulin as needed
  5. Dose adjustment: Consider dose reduction if hyperglycemia severe and uncontrolled

Important note: In Cushing’s disease, glucose metabolism may initially worsen (due to pasireotide) then improve as cortisol normalizes. Net glycemic effect varies.

Contraindications

  1. Known hypersensitivity to pasireotide or excipients
  2. Severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh C)

Precautions

  3. Cholelithiasis history — Ultrasound monitoring recommended

Dosing Considerations

Cushing’s disease (SC formulation):

  • Starting dose: 600 mcg or 900 mcg SC twice daily
  • Titration: Based on UFC response and tolerability
  • Maximum: 900 mcg twice daily

Acromegaly (LAR formulation):

  • Starting dose: 40 mg IM every 28 days
  • Titration: May increase to 60 mg based on response
  • Maximum: 60 mg every 28 days

When to Choose Pasireotide

Consider pasireotide when:

  1. Patient has Cushing’s disease (medical therapy indicated)
  2. Acromegaly inadequately controlled on octreotide/lanreotide
  3. Tumor likely high SSTR5 expression (e.g., corticotroph adenoma)
  4. Second-line or third-line medical therapy needed

Consider first-generation SSA (octreotide/lanreotide) when:

  1. Treatment-naive acromegaly (unless contraindicated)
  2. Patient has pre-existing diabetes with poor control
  3. Cost is a significant consideration
  4. Lower metabolic risk profile preferred
