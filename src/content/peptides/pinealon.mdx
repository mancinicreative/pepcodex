<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

## Quick Summary

- **Synthetic tripeptide** (Glutamic acid-Aspartic acid-Arginine / Glu-Asp-Arg / EDR)
- **Developed in Russia** by Vladimir Khavinson at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology
- **Claimed uses:** Cognitive enhancement, neuroprotection, brain aging
- **Part of "peptide bioregulator" program** - short peptides claimed to regulate gene expression in specific tissues
- **NOT approved** by FDA, EMA, or any Western regulatory agency
- **Evidence:** Almost exclusively Russian publications; no independent Western validation

**Evidence Strength: VERY LOW** — Claims are based primarily on Russian research from a single laboratory group with no independent Western replication. Extraordinary claims about gene regulation and neuroprotection lack rigorous validation.

### Theoretical Basis: Peptide Bioregulation

Khavinson's theory proposes that:

```
Short peptides (2-4 amino acids)
    |
    v
Enter cell nucleus
    |
    v
Bind specific DNA sequences
    |
    v
Regulate gene expression
    |
    v
"Restore" tissue function
```

**Critical assessment:** This theory is not accepted by mainstream molecular biology. The claimed mechanism would be extraordinary and requires extraordinary evidence that has not been provided.

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>

Pinealon represents:

**What Is Claimed:**
- Synthetic tripeptide that regulates brain gene expression
- Neuroprotective and cognitive-enhancing effects
- Part of a "peptide bioregulator" paradigm
- Derived from Russian gerontology research

**What We Actually Know:**
- A synthetic tripeptide (Glu-Asp-Arg)
- Research comes almost exclusively from one Russian laboratory
- No independent Western replication exists
- Claimed mechanism is not accepted by mainstream science
- No rigorous human clinical trials

**Critical Problems:**
- Single laboratory source for all positive data
- Extraordinary claims without extraordinary evidence
- Mechanism (DNA binding by tripeptide) is implausible
- No pharmacokinetic data
- Products are unregulated research chemicals

**Evidence Reality Check:**
- The "very low" rating reflects genuine scientific uncertainty
- This is not "low evidence that it works" but "almost no credible evidence at all"
- The peptide may have no effect beyond amino acid nutrition
- Even if effects exist, they may be completely different from claims

**Bottom Line:** Pinealon represents the extreme end of unvalidated peptide research. While the Khavinson laboratory has published extensively, the lack of any independent Western replication, the implausible mechanism, and the methodological limitations of the Russian studies mean there is essentially no credible evidence for the claimed effects. Consumers should understand they are relying entirely on unvalidated claims from a single interested source.
