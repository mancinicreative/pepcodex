## Overview

<h2 id="overview" class="sr-only">Overview</h2>

### Quick Summary

- **Melanocortin-4 receptor agonist** that acts on central nervous system
- **FDA approved** (2019) as Vyleesi for HSDD in premenopausal women
- **First CNS-acting approved treatment** for female sexual dysfunction
- **On-demand dosing** - taken before anticipated sexual activity
- **Derived from Melanotan II** but without tanning effects at approved level

**Evidence Strength: High** - FDA approval based on Phase 3 trials; clear efficacy signal for indicated population.

<h2 id="mechanism-of-action">Mechanism of Action</h2>

### How PT-141 Works

Unlike Viagra and similar drugs that work by improving blood flow to genitals, PT-141 works in the **brain**. It activates receptors in a part of the brain called the hypothalamus that controls sexual desire and arousal. This is why PT-141 can increase **desire** itself, not just physical response.

Think of it this way: Viagra helps the body respond to desire that's already there. PT-141 may help generate that desire in the first place by triggering the release of dopamine (the "wanting" chemical) in brain regions that control sexual motivation.

This brain-based mechanism is also why PT-141 is the **first FDA-approved treatment for low sexual desire in women**. Previous drugs that worked on blood flow had minimal effect on female sexual function.

---

### Melanocortin Receptor Binding

PT-141 (bremelanotide) is a cyclic heptapeptide derived from alpha-MSH that activates **multiple melanocortin receptors** with the following potency order:

| Receptor | Relative Potency | Key Tissue Expression |
|----------|------------------|----------------------|
| MC1R | Highest | Skin melanocytes (tanning) |
| **MC4R** | High | **Hypothalamus (sexual function)** |
| MC3R | Moderate | Hypothalamus, limbic system |
| MC5R | Lower | Exocrine glands |
| MC2R | Lowest | Adrenal cortex |

**MC4R is the most relevant** at therapeutic doses for sexual function.

### Central Neural Pathways

```
PT-141 crosses blood-brain barrier
    |
Binds MC4R in medial preoptic area (mPOA) of hypothalamus
    |
Presynaptic MC4R activation
    |
Increased dopamine release in mPOA
    |
|-- Increased sexual desire
|-- Increased genital arousal (via spinal pathways)
|-- Oxytocin pathway activation
```

**Downstream Effects:**
- **Dopaminergic activation**: Reward and motivation circuits
- **Oxytocinergic activation**: Bonding and arousal pathways
- **Spinal cord effects**: Direct pro-erectile signaling via MC4R

### Brain Region Activation

Animal studies show PT-141 activates specific brain regions:

| Region | Role | Evidence |
|--------|------|----------|
| Medial preoptic area (mPOA) | Sexual behavior coordination | Increased FOS immunostaining |
| Paraventricular nucleus (PVN) | Neural activation | FOS expression |
| Ventral tegmental area (VTA) | Dopamine reward | Increased activity |

### Why It Works Differently Than Viagra

| Aspect | PT-141 | PDE5 Inhibitors (Viagra) |
|--------|--------|--------------------------|
| Target | **Central (brain)** | Peripheral (blood vessels) |
| Primary effect | Increased sexual **desire** | Improved erectile **function** |
| Mechanism | MC4R to dopamine | NO to cGMP to vasodilation |
| Sexual stimulation | Works without | Requires |
| Works in women | **FDA approved** | No significant effect |

### Intracellular Signaling

MC4R activation triggers:
```
PT-141 binds MC4R (GPCR)
    |
Gs-alpha activation
    |
Adenylyl cyclase
    |
Increased cAMP
    |
Downstream signaling
```

This cAMP increase in hypothalamic neurons modulates neurotransmitter release.

<h2 id="key-human-trials">Key Human Trials</h2>

### RECONNECT Phase 3 Trials

**Population:** Premenopausal women with HSDD

| Trial | N | Duration | Primary Outcome |
|-------|---|----------|-----------------|
| RECONNECT 1 (NCT02333071) | 634 | 24 weeks | Significant improvement in desire |
| RECONNECT 2 (NCT02338960) | 636 | 24 weeks | Significant improvement in desire |

### Phase 3 Key Findings

- **FSFI-D Score:** Statistically significant improvement vs placebo
- **FSDS-DAO Score:** Reduced distress related to low desire
- **Satisfying Sexual Events:** Modest but significant increase
- **Effect Size:** Clinically meaningful but moderate

### Long-Term Extension Study

| Study | N | Duration | Key Findings |
|-------|---|----------|--------------|
| Open-label extension (NCT02516891) | 684 | 52 weeks | Sustained efficacy; no new safety signals |

### Earlier Phase 2 Studies

| Study | Year | Population | Key Findings |
|-------|------|------------|--------------|
| Diamond et al. (PMID:16839319) | 2006 | Women with sexual arousal disorder | Improved subjective sexual response |
| Safarinejad (PMID:18221289) | 2008 | Female arousal disorder | SC administration effective |
| Clayton et al. (PMID:27137527) | 2016 | Premenopausal HSDD | 1.75mg dose identified as optimal |

### Important Context

- Approved specifically for premenopausal women
- Not approved for men (early trials showed less consistent results)
- On-demand use, not daily

<h2 id="safety-profile">Safety Profile</h2>

### Clinical Trial Adverse Events

| Event | Incidence | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Nausea | 40% | Most common; often transient |
| Flushing | 21% | Expected from mechanism |
| Headache | 11% | Usually mild |
| Injection site reaction | 13% | Local effects |

### Safety Analysis Studies

| Study | Year | Key Findings |
|-------|------|--------------|
| Krop et al. (PMID:30448051) | 2018 | Transient BP increases; no CV events in trials |
| Portman et al. (PMID:31877090) | 2020 | Pooled Phase 3 safety; nausea most common |
| Clayton et al. (PMID:32443076) | 2020 | Nausea characterization; diminishes with use |

### Warnings and Precautions

- **Blood pressure:** Transient increases observed
- **Nausea:** Can be significant; limits use
- **Cardiovascular:** Caution in uncontrolled hypertension
- **Focal hyperpigmentation:** Possible with repeated use

### Contraindications

- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Known cardiovascular disease
- Concomitant use with naltrexone (interaction)
