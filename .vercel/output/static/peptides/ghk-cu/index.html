GHK-Cu

Also known as: Copper Peptide GHK, Glycyl-L-Histidyl-L-Lysine:Copper

Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Naturally occurring tripeptide (Gly-His-Lys) that binds copper
  • Endogenous to human plasma - levels decline with age
  • Primarily topical use in cosmetic and wound healing applications
  • Moderate-to-high evidence for skin regeneration and wound healing
  • Found in many skincare products as active ingredient
  • Extensively researched globally - USA, China, South Korea, Japan, Europe

Evidence Strength: Moderate-High - Strong evidence base from multiple RCTs, systematic reviews, and meta-analysis for topical wound healing and cosmetic applications.

Natural Occurrence

GHK-Cu is found naturally in human plasma, urine, and saliva. Plasma levels decline significantly with age (200 ng/mL at age 20 to 80 ng/mL at age 60).

Mechanism of Action

How GHK-Cu Works

GHK-Cu is a small molecule that your body naturally produces, and it acts like a master switch for tissue repair and regeneration. Here’s what it does:

  1. Signals cells to repair and regenerate - When tissue is damaged, GHK-Cu is released and acts as a “help signal” that attracts repair cells to the injury site, similar to a beacon calling in construction crews.

  2. Boosts collagen production - Collagen is the protein that gives skin its structure and firmness. With age, collagen production slows. GHK-Cu can increase collagen production by up to 70% in lab studies.

  3. Provides copper for healing enzymes - The copper in GHK-Cu isn’t just decoration. Copper is essential for enzymes that crosslink collagen fibers together, making them stronger. Without copper, these enzymes don’t work.

  4. Protects against oxidative damage - GHK-Cu neutralizes harmful reactive molecules that damage cells and accelerate aging.

Why it matters: Your body’s GHK-Cu levels drop significantly with age, from about 200 ng/mL at age 20 to only 80 ng/mL by age 60. This decline may partly explain why older skin heals more slowly and shows more signs of aging.

Gene Expression Modulation

GHK-Cu is capable of up- and downregulating at least 4,000 human genes, essentially resetting DNA expression toward a healthier state. This is a remarkably broad effect for such a small molecule.

Wound Healing Cascade

When tissue is injured, GHK-Cu is naturally released from damaged collagen by a protein called SPARC. It then acts as a chemical signal that attracts repair cells to the wound:

Cellular Recruitment:

Tissue Injury -> SPARC protein releases GHK from collagen
    |
GHK-Cu acts as chemoattractant
    |
    |-- Mast cells recruited (immune response)
    |-- Macrophages recruited (clean up debris)
    |-- Fibroblasts migrate (produce new collagen)
    |-- Endothelial cells migrate (form new blood vessels)
    |
Tissue repair proteins released

Collagen Production:

  • Increases collagen production up to 70% in laboratory studies
  • Enhances Type I collagen (provides structural support) and Type III collagen (provides flexibility during repair)
  • Copper serves as essential cofactor for lysyl oxidase and lysyl hydroxylase, enzymes that crosslink collagen fibers together like rebar reinforcing concrete

Essential Role of Copper

The copper ion (Cu2+) is required for biological activity. GHK without copper doesn’t show the same healing effects:

  • Lysyl oxidase - Crosslinks collagen and elastin fibers together for strength
  • Superoxide dismutase (SOD) - Neutralizes damaging free radicals
  • Cytochrome c oxidase - Powers cellular energy production
  • Only GHK-Cu, not GHK alone, shows wound healing effects

Antioxidant Properties

GHK-Cu acts as a powerful antioxidant, protecting cells from oxidative stress:

  • Blocks formation of reactive oxygen/carbonyl species (the harmful molecules that damage DNA and proteins)
  • Detoxifies toxic lipid peroxidation products (acrolein), byproducts of fat breakdown that cause cellular damage
  • Protects skin cells (keratinocytes) from lethal UVB radiation damage
  • Blocks liver damage from toxic chemical radicals

Natural Source & Decline with Age

GHK-Cu is released from collagen after tissue injury via SPARC protein.

  • Plasma levels: approximately 200 ng/mL at age 20, dropping to approximately 80 ng/mL at age 60 (a 60% decline)
  • This age-related decline may explain why older skin heals more slowly and is more prone to damage

Pharmacokinetic Limitations

  • Half-life: ~30 minutes in plasma
  • Skin absorption: Poor due to high hydrophilicity
  • Clearance: ~95% excreted rapidly after dermal injection

Pharmacokinetic Challenges

GHK-Cu has inherent delivery challenges:

  • High hydrophilicity limits skin penetration
  • Short plasma half-life (~30 minutes)
  • Advanced delivery systems address these limitations
