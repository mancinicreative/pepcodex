Overview

Overview

Quick Summary

  • GHRH(1-29) analog — the biologically active portion of GHRH
  • Was FDA approved (1997) for pediatric GH deficiency as Geref
  • Discontinued commercially (2008) for business reasons, not safety
  • Preserves physiologic GH pulsatility unlike direct GH administration
  • Served as foundation for newer GHRH analogs

Evidence Strength: Moderate — Was approved with Phase 3 data; discontinued for commercial reasons rather than efficacy or safety concerns.

  • Current: Available through compounding pharmacies

Mechanism of Action

How Sermorelin Works

Sermorelin is essentially a shortened, synthetic version of the hormone your brain uses to tell your pituitary gland to release growth hormone. Here’s what it does:

  1. Mimics your natural signal - Your brain produces GHRH (Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone) to trigger growth hormone release. Sermorelin is the active part of that hormone (amino acids 1-29), so your body responds to it like the real thing.

  2. Works through your pituitary - Unlike injecting growth hormone directly, sermorelin asks your pituitary gland to release its own growth hormone. This means your body’s natural feedback systems stay intact.

  3. Preserves your natural rhythm - Your body releases growth hormone in pulses, especially during sleep. Sermorelin maintains this pulsatile pattern rather than flooding your system with constant GH levels.

  4. Requires a working pituitary - Because sermorelin works by stimulating your pituitary gland, it only works if your pituitary can still produce growth hormone. This is why it was approved for children with GH deficiency (whose pituitaries still functioned) but not for adults with severe pituitary damage.

Development note: Sermorelin was FDA-approved in 1997 for pediatric GH deficiency but was discontinued in 2008 for business reasons (low sales, manufacturing costs) - not because of safety concerns.

GHRH Receptor and cAMP/PKA Signaling

Sermorelin is the biologically active 1-29 amino acid fragment of endogenous GHRH. It binds to the GHRH receptor (GHRH-R), a G protein-coupled receptor on pituitary somatotroph cells.

Primary Signaling Cascade:

Sermorelin binds GHRH-R (GPCR)
    |
Conformational change -> Gs protein activation
    |
Adenylyl Cyclase stimulation
    |
ATP -> cAMP (second messenger)
    |
Protein Kinase A (PKA) activation
    |
+-- GH gene transcription (up)
+-- GH synthesis (up)
+-- GH vesicle exocytosis

Secondary Pathway (PLC/IP3/DAG):

  • Contributes to release of pre-synthesized GH
  • Increases intracellular calcium levels
  • Results in pulsatile GH release

GHRH Receptor Isoforms

Two isoforms exist with different signaling properties:

  • Short isoform: Predominant in pituitary; signals through cAMP
  • Long isoform: Binds GHRH but does not activate cAMP pathway

Cross-talk with Other Pathways

GHRH receptor activation also engages:

  • MAPK/ERK pathway: Cell survival and differentiation
  • PI3K/Akt pathway: Transcriptional outputs

Physiologic GH Release

Unlike exogenous GH:

  • Preserves negative feedback via somatostatin
  • Maintains pulsatile secretion (mimics natural rhythm)
  • GH and IGF-1 remain in physiologic range
  • Somatostatin prevents overdose and tachyphylaxis
  • Requires functional pituitary to work

Key Human Trials

Discovery and Early Development

Guillemin R et al. (1982) — PMID:6142044

  • Journal: Science
  • Significance: Original discovery and characterization of GHRH; identified 1-29 as biologically active fragment (Nobel Prize work)

Ross RJ et al. (1984) — PMID:6092099

  • Journal: The Lancet (UK)
  • Design: Early proof-of-concept in pediatric GH deficiency
  • Results: First demonstration of growth acceleration with GHRH therapy

Registration Trials (Pediatric GH Deficiency)

Geref International Study Group (1996) — Thorner M et al. PMID:8772599

  • Design: Multicenter open-label study, N=110 prepubertal GH-deficient children (USA, France, Italy)
  • Intervention: 30 mcg/kg sermorelin SC at bedtime for up to 1 year
  • Results: 74% of children showed increased growth rate after 6 months; significant increases in height velocity sustained through 12 months
EndpointFinding
GH releaseSignificant stimulation
Growth velocityImproved vs baseline
IGF-1Appropriate increase

De Zegher F et al. (1987) — PMID:3297423

  • Design: Multicenter European study (Belgium, Netherlands, Germany)
  • Population: GH-deficient children
  • Results: Established European reference ranges for GHRH diagnostic testing

Adult/Aging Studies (Somatopause Research)

Corpas E et al. (1992) — NIH PMID:1379256

  • Design: Controlled trial in 9 young men (22-33 yr) and 10 elderly men (60-78 yr)
  • Intervention: GHRH(1-29) 5 mcg/kg twice daily for 14 days
  • Results: Reversed age-related decreases in GH and IGF-1 levels in elderly men

Khorram O et al. (1997) — PMID:9141536

  • Design: 5-month RCT, N=19 participants aged 55-71 years
  • Intervention: Nle27-GHRH(1-29)NH2 daily
  • Results: Significant increases in nocturnal GH and serum IGF-1; men showed improvements in insulin sensitivity, libido, and quality of life

Merriam GR et al. (2005) — PMID:16099970

  • Design: 6-month RCT, N=89 healthy older adults (65-88 years)
  • Results: GHRH increased IGF-1 and lean mass; fat mass decreased; no major adverse effects

Vittone J et al. (1997) — NIH PMID:9475094

  • Design: 8-month dose-ranging study in GH-deficient adults
  • Results: Sustained efficacy and safety with graded GHRH doses

Sleep and Cognitive Function Studies

Vitiello MV et al. (2006) — PMID:17047218

  • Design: RCT, N=89 healthy older adults
  • Journal: Journal of the American Geriatrics Society
  • Results: GHRH improved sleep quality and cognitive function; enhanced quality of life measures

Friedman SD et al. (2013) — PMID:23588422 — NCT00257712

  • Design: 20-week RCT, N=30 adults (55-87 yr), 17 with mild cognitive impairment
  • Intervention: GHRH (sermorelin acetate) for 20 weeks
  • Results: Increased brain GABA levels in all regions; improved performance on tests of fluid intelligence (working memory, planning, selective attention, processing speed)

Frieboes RM et al. (2000) — Germany PMID:10720539

  • Design: Human study in healthy young adults
  • Results: Sermorelin enhanced slow-wave sleep and GH secretion without affecting REM sleep

Marshall L et al. (1993) — Germany PMID:8432877

  • Design: Human study in healthy young adults
  • Results: GHRH administration increased slow-wave sleep and decreased wakefulness

European Research

Iovino M et al. (1989) — Italy PMID:2106347

  • Population: Young and elderly adults
  • Results: Demonstrated preserved pituitary responsiveness to GHRH(1-29) in aging; age-related GH decline is hypothalamic, not pituitary

Lang I et al. (1988) — Austria PMID:3135205

  • Population: Elderly men (65-80 years)
  • Results: GHRH(1-29) effectively stimulates GH release in elderly; response magnitude correlates with baseline IGF-1

Cacciari E et al. (1989) — Italy PMID:2556728

  • Population: GH-deficient children
  • Results: Established GHRH test diagnostic criteria; distinguished hypothalamic from pituitary GH deficiency

Aimaretti G et al. (1998) — Italy PMID:8838696

  • Design: Diagnostic study in adults with suspected GH deficiency
  • Results: GHRH+arginine test validation; established as gold standard diagnostic in adults

Ghigo E et al. (1996) — Italy PMID:8774234

  • Results: Demonstrated distinct mechanisms of GHRH vs GHRPs; confirmed additive/synergistic effects

Japanese and Russian Research

Shibasaki T et al. (1989) — Japan PMID:2574461

  • Population: Healthy elderly and young adults
  • Results: Chronic GHRH administration increases IGF-1 in elderly; demonstrates sustained efficacy without tachyphylaxis

Dedov II et al. (1989) — Russia/USSR PMID:2676238

  • Population: GH-deficient patients (USSR cohort)
  • Results: Soviet research confirming GHRH diagnostic utility; population-specific response data

Special Populations

Rolla M et al. (1987) — Italy PMID:3104139

  • Population: Depression and anorexia nervosa patients
  • Results: Abnormal GH response to GHRH in depression; preserved response in anorexia nervosa

Press M et al. (1987) — UK PMID:2892214

  • Population: Type 1 diabetics and controls
  • Results: Altered GH response pattern in Type 1 diabetes; implications for diabetic growth retardation

Mauss S et al. (1996) — Germany PMID:8954015

  • Population: HIV patients with lipodystrophy
  • Results: GHRH testing revealed GH axis abnormalities in HIV lipodystrophy; precursor to tesamorelin research

Mericq V et al. (2001) — Chile PMID:11502770

  • Population: Peripubertal children with GH neurosecretory dysfunction
  • Results: GHRH testing distinguishes hypothalamic from pituitary dysfunction

Diagnostic Use

Sermorelin stimulation test was used to assess pituitary GH reserve, distinguishing GH deficiency from normal.
