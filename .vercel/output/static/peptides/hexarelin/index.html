Hexarelin

Also known as: Examorelin, HEX, Growth Hormone Releasing Hexapeptide

The most potent synthetic GHRP (Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide), a hexapeptide that strongly stimulates GH release via the ghrelin receptor. Notable for cardioprotective effects independent of GH release. Development discontinued due to rapid desensitization with repeated dosing. Italian research leads global investigation.

Overview

Quick Summary

  • Most potent synthetic growth hormone secretagogue in the GHRP family
  • Hexapeptide structure: His-D-2-methyl-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys-NH2
  • Not FDA approved for any indication; Phase 2 development discontinued
  • Unique cardioprotective effects independent of GH release—acts directly on cardiac tissue
  • Rapid desensitization: GH response significantly diminishes within days of repeated dosing
  • Moderate selectivity: Some cortisol and prolactin elevation, but less than GHRP-6/GHRP-2
  • Italian research leadership: University of Turin (Ghigo, Arvat) produced most human clinical data

Evidence Strength: MODERATE — Extensive human pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic studies exist demonstrating potent GH release and cardioprotective effects. However, clinical development was discontinued due to desensitization, and no efficacy trials for clinical endpoints were completed.

Why Hexarelin is the “Most Potent” GHRP

Hexarelin earned this designation through systematic comparative studies:

  1. Structure optimization: Addition of 2-methyl group to D-Trp enhanced receptor binding
  2. GH release magnitude: Produces 2-3x higher GH peaks than GHRP-6 at equivalent doses
  3. Potency confirmation: Multiple head-to-head human studies confirmed superiority
  4. Italian leadership: Mediolanum Pharmaceuticals and University of Turin collaborated on development

The potency paradox: Despite being the most potent GHRP, hexarelin’s rapid desensitization made it unsuitable for chronic therapy—the very situation where GH secretagogues would be most useful.

Mechanism of Action

How Hexarelin Works

Hexarelin triggers growth hormone release by activating the ghrelin receptor, but it also has unique direct effects on the heart. Here’s what makes it different:

  1. Activates the ghrelin receptor - Like other GHRPs, hexarelin binds to GHS-R1a receptors on pituitary cells and triggers GH release. It does this more powerfully than any other synthetic GHRP.

  2. Causes potent GH release - A single dose produces very high GH peaks, typically 2-3 times higher than GHRP-6. This was exciting for researchers initially.

  3. Desensitizes rapidly - The major limitation: after just a few days of repeated dosing, the GH response drops dramatically. By day 4-7, responses are often 50-80% lower than the initial dose. This is called tachyphylaxis.

  4. Has direct heart effects - Uniquely among GHRPs, hexarelin appears to protect heart tissue through mechanisms that don’t require GH release at all. This acts through CD36 receptors on cardiac cells.

Why development stopped: The rapid desensitization meant hexarelin couldn’t maintain GH elevation with chronic use, making it impractical for treating GH deficiency or aging. However, the cardiac protective effects remain an area of research interest.

Comparison to family members: Hexarelin is more potent but desensitizes faster than ipamorelin, GHRP-6, or GHRP-2. This appears to be related to its higher receptor affinity causing more rapid receptor downregulation.

Receptor Targets

Primary: Ghrelin Receptor (GHS-R1a)

Receptor Classification (IUPHAR/BPS Guide to Pharmacology):

  • Official name: Ghrelin receptor (GHSR)
  • Gene: GHSR (human)
  • Class: Class A G protein-coupled receptor (7 transmembrane domains)
  • Endogenous ligand: Ghrelin (pKd 8.6-9.4)
  • Hexarelin binding affinity: High affinity agonist; pEC50 approximately 8.5
  • Constitutive activity: Yes - receptor maintains basal activity without ligand

Primary Signaling (IUPHAR):

  • Transduction: Gq/G11-mediated phospholipase C stimulation
  • Downstream: IP3 accumulation, intracellular Ca2+ release
  • Additional: ERK phosphorylation, beta-arrestin signaling

Secondary: CD36 (Cardiac Scavenger Receptor)

This is hexarelin’s unique contribution to GHRP research:

  • CD36 is expressed on cardiomyocytes
  • Hexarelin binding activates protective signaling independent of GHS-R1a
  • Mechanism involves PPAR-gamma activation and anti-apoptotic pathways
  • May explain cardioprotection observed even when GH response is desensitized

Desensitization Mechanism

The rapid tachyphylaxis that limited hexarelin’s development:

Day 1: Full GH response (100%)
    ↓
Days 2-3: GHS-R1a receptor internalization begins
    ↓
Days 4-7: 50-80% reduction in GH response
    ↓
Mechanism: Receptor downregulation + reduced receptor recycling
    ↓
Result: Chronic dosing ineffective for GH elevation

Why hexarelin desensitizes faster than other GHRPs:

  • Higher receptor affinity = more rapid receptor occupancy
  • Stronger receptor activation = more pronounced internalization
  • Possible unique receptor conformation effects

Key Evidence Highlights

  1. Potency: Confirmed as most potent synthetic GH secretagogue in human studies
  2. Desensitization: GH response decreases 50-80% within one week of daily dosing
  3. Cardioprotection: Direct cardiac effects independent of GH documented
  4. Selectivity: Less cortisol/prolactin elevation than GHRP-6/GHRP-2, but more than ipamorelin
  5. Development outcome: Discontinued due to desensitization preventing chronic use

Desensitization as a Safety Consideration

The rapid desensitization actually limited safety concerns:

  • Short-term exposure = limited long-term safety data needed
  • Self-limiting effect = abuse potential theoretically lower

WADA Status

Hexarelin is PROHIBITED under the World Anti-Doping Agency code.

  • Listed under S2: Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors, Related Substances and Mimetics
  • Specifically: “Growth Hormone Secretagogues (GHS)”
  • Banned both in-competition and out-of-competition
  • Detection methods available via mass spectrometry
  • Specific urinary metabolite detection developed (German researchers)

Anti-Doping Detection

Thomas A et al. developed LC-MS/MS methods for hexarelin detection in urine, enabling enforcement of WADA prohibition.

Why Development Was Discontinued

  1. Rapid desensitization: GH response decreased 50-80% within one week
  2. No sustained IGF-1 elevation: Primary endpoint for GH deficiency indications
  3. Competing compounds: Ipamorelin (less desensitization), tesamorelin (GHRH analog) advanced
  4. Limited chronic utility: Desensitization prevented long-term therapeutic use
  5. Cardiac potential unrealized: GH-independent effects not pursued clinically

Why Hexarelin’s Cardioprotection is Unique

Among all GHRPs, hexarelin has the most extensive cardiac research:

  1. Largest body of evidence for direct cardiac effects
  2. GH-independent mechanism best characterized
  3. CD36 receptor interaction most thoroughly studied
  4. Clinical cardiac effects documented in human studies
  5. Italian research leadership produced systematic investigation

This makes hexarelin interesting for potential cardiac applications even though it failed as a GH secretagogue.

Why Hexarelin Remains of Research Interest

Despite failed development, hexarelin contributes to understanding:

  1. GHS receptor desensitization - Model compound for receptor downregulation
  2. GH-independent GHRP effects - Separating cardiac from hormonal actions
  3. CD36 signaling - Novel cardioprotective pathway
  4. Structure-activity relationships - What makes a potent GH secretagogue
